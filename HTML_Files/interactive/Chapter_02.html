Chapter Opener: A Hospital, a Highway, and a Lost Spacecraft

In 1999, NASA's Mars Climate Orbiter — a $125-million mission to study the Martian atmosphere — burned up in the upper atmosphere of Mars and was destroyed. The cause was not a software bug, a manufacturing defect, or a hostile asteroid. The cause was that one engineering team supplied the spacecraft's thruster forces in pound-seconds while the navigation team's software expected newton-seconds. The numbers were correct; the units were wrong; the spacecraft was lost. Closer to home, every nurse who measures a pediatric medication dose works with the same kind of arithmetic: a number, a unit, a conversion.

This chapter teaches you a single procedure — dimensional analysis — that catches errors like NASA's before they leave the lab notebook. Mastering it is the most important quantitative skill in introductory chemistry. The same procedure also lets you compute densities, convert between metric and English units, change temperature scales, and calculate percent error. By the end of the chapter you will treat units as full-fledged citizens of every calculation, never strangers to be dropped at the end.

Why this matters: Dimensional analysis is one of the most-used and much-needed number-manipulation techniques in chemistry. Every quantitative problem in Chapters 8, 9, and 10 — and every quantitative problem in general chemistry, organic chemistry, biochemistry, and physical chemistry that follows — is, at the level of arithmetic, a dimensional-analysis problem. The same factor-label procedure is what a nurse uses to verify a drug dose, what an engineer uses to convert specifications between SI and customary units, and what a researcher uses to check that a published result is dimensionally sane. Master it here and you will spend the rest of your science career applying it, not relearning it.

2.1 The Metric System — SI Units and Prefixes

The Système International d'Unités, abbreviated SI, is the worldwide standard system of measurement. Every scientific paper, every drug-dosage label, and every modern engineering specification uses it. SI is built on seven base units, listed in Table 2.1. 2.2 Metric Units of Length

Length is the distance between two points; the SI base unit is the meter (m). For everyday chemistry the meter is convenient for laboratory benches, the centimeter (cm) for glassware and small objects, the millimeter (mm) for capillary tubes and crystal sizes, and the nanometer (nm) for atoms and molecules. A typical bond length in an organic molecule is about 0.15 nm; a hydrogen atom is about 0.1 nm across; a typical bacterium is several micrometers long.

Useful conversions to memorize: 1 m = 100 cm = 1000 mm; 1 cm = 10 mm; 1 km = 1000 m. From these three relationships every other length conversion follows.

2.3 Metric Units of Mass

Mass is the amount of matter in a sample. The SI base unit is the kilogram (kg), defined since 2019 by fixing the value of Planck's constant. The gram (g, 10⁻³ kg) is more common in chemistry; the milligram (mg, 10⁻³ g) is common in pharmacy; the microgram (µg) is common in trace analysis.

CAUTION: Mass and weight are not the same. Mass is the amount of matter; weight is the gravitational force on that matter. A 1.000 kg sample has the same mass on the Moon as on Earth, but its weight on the Moon is about one-sixth of its weight on Earth. Chemistry calculations use mass.

2.4 Metric Units of Volume

Volume is the amount of space a sample occupies. The SI derived unit is the cubic meter (m³); 1 m³ is the volume of a cube one meter on a side. Chemistry rarely deals with that much liquid at once, so we use the liter (L), defined as exactly 1 dm³ (1 cubic decimeter). 1 L = 1000 mL = 1000 cm³, and 1 mL = 1 cm³ exactly. The mL/cm³ identity is one of the most useful relationships in introductory chemistry; memorize it.

Common laboratory volumes range from a few µL (a single drop from an automatic pipette) to a few L (a beaker for solution preparation). Industrial chemistry can reach m³ or even thousands of m³.

2.5 Units in Mathematical Operations

Units obey the same algebra as variables. They cancel by division, multiply through products, and never appear in the final answer except where they belong physically.

  • Multiplication: (5 m) × (3 m) = 15 m². The unit m² is the unit of area.
  • Division: (60 km) ÷ (2 h) = 30 km/h. The slash means "per".
  • Cancellation: (12 g/mol) × (3 mol) = 36 g; the mol cancels.

If a calculation comes out with the wrong units, the calculation is wrong. Watching the units is the cheapest, fastest error check available.

2.6 Conversion Factors

A conversion factor is a fraction whose numerator and denominator describe the same amount in two different units. Because top and bottom are the same physical amount, the factor equals 1 — and you can multiply any quantity by 1 without changing what it represents. That is the entire mathematical justification for what follows.

Every relationship of the form "A = B" yields two conversion factors:

conversion factor = (B / A) or (A / B)

English-to-English Factors

Examples: 12 in = 1 ft → factors are (12 in / 1 ft) and (1 ft / 12 in). 16 oz = 1 lb → (16 oz / 1 lb) and (1 lb / 16 oz). 1 mi = 5280 ft → (5280 ft / 1 mi) and (1 mi / 5280 ft).

Metric-to-Metric Factors

All metric-to-metric factors come from the prefix definitions in Table 2.2: 1 km = 10³ m, 1 mg = 10⁻³ g, 1 µL = 10⁻⁶ L, etc.

2.7 Dimensional Analysis (Factor-Label Method)

The factor-label method, also called dimensional analysis, is a systematic way to use conversion factors. Three rules:

  1. Write down the given quantity with its units.
  2. Multiply by a conversion factor arranged so that the unit you want to cancel sits OPPOSITE on the new factor (top cancels bottom or bottom cancels top).
  3. Repeat until only the desired final units remain.

The whole calculation looks like a chain of fractions stretching across the page; the units march off step by step until only the desired one is left.

EXAMPLE 2.1: Metric-to-metric (single step)

Convert 4.50 km to meters.

Step 1 — Given: 4.50 km. Wanted: m.

Step 2 — Conversion factor: 1 km = 10³ m → use (10³ m / 1 km) so km cancels.

\[\text{Step 3 — Set up :}\ 4.50 \cancel{\text{km}} \times \dfrac{10^{3} \text{m}}{1 \cancel{\text{km}}} = 4.50 \times 10^{3} \text{m}.\]

Units cancel as marked: "km" cancels against the conversion factor, leaving "m". Three sig figs from "4.50 km".

Step 4 — Sig figs: input has 3, factor is exact → 3 sig figs.

Show solution

4.50 × 10³ m (= 4500 m).

EXAMPLE 2.2: English-to-English (single step)

Convert 75.0 ft to inches.

Step 1 — Given: 75.0 ft. Wanted: in.

Step 2 — 1 ft = 12 in (exact).

\[Step 3 — 75.0 \cancel{\text{ft}} \times \dfrac{12 \text{in}}{1 \cancel{\text{ft}}} = 900. \text{in}\ \text{(the period denotes the exact 3-sig-fig result).}\]

"ft" cancels, leaving "in". The 12 in/ft factor is exact, so the input "75.0 ft" sets the 3 sig figs.

Show solution

900. in.

EXAMPLE 2.3: English-to-metric (single step)

A patient weighs 165 lb. What is the patient's mass in kilograms?

Step 1 — Given: 165 lb. Wanted: kg.

Step 2 — 1 lb = 453.59 g, and 1 kg = 10³ g.

\[Step 3 — 165 \cancel{\text{lb}} \times \dfrac{453.59 \cancel{\text{g}}}{1 \cancel{\text{lb}}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{kg}}{10^{3} \cancel{\text{g}}} = 74.84 \text{kg}.\]

Two cancellations: "lb" in step 1, "g" in step 2. Three sig figs from "165 lb" govern the answer.

Step 4 — Sig figs: 165 has 3, 453.59 has 5, 10³ is exact → limit 3 → 74.8 kg.

Show solution

74.8 kg.

EXAMPLE 2.4: Multi-step conversion

A car is traveling at 65.0 mi/h. What is its speed in m/s?

Step 1 — Given: 65.0 mi/h. Wanted: m/s.

Step 2 — Build a chain: mi → km → m, and h → min → s.

\[Step 3 — 65.0 \cancel{\text{mi}}/\cancel{\text{h}} \times \dfrac{1.609 \cancel{\text{km}}}{1 \cancel{\text{mi}}} \times \dfrac{10^{3} \text{m}}{1 \cancel{\text{km}}} \times \dfrac{1 \cancel{\text{h}}}{60 \cancel{\text{min}}} \times \dfrac{1 \cancel{\text{min}}}{60 \text{s}} = ?\]

Four pairs cancel: "mi", "km", "h", "min" — leaving "m" in the numerator and "s" in the denominator (m/s).

Step 4 — Numerator: 65.0 × 1.609 × 10³ = 1.046 × 10⁵ m·(unit chain). Denominator: 60 × 60 = 3600 s.

Step 5 — 1.046 × 10⁵ / 3.600 × 10³ = 29.05 m/s.

Step 6 — Sig figs: 3 (limited by 65.0) → 29.1 m/s.

Show solution

29.1 m/s.

CHECK: 65 mph is roughly 100 km/h, and 100 km/h is roughly 28 m/s. ✓

2.8 Density

Density (symbol d or ρ) is the ratio of a sample's mass to its volume:

d = m / V

Density is an intensive property: it does not depend on how much sample you have. A 1.0 mL drop of liquid water and a 1.0 L flask of liquid water have the same density (1.00 g/mL at 4 °C). Densities are used to identify substances, distinguish pure samples from impure ones, and convert between mass and volume — exactly the job of a conversion factor.

EXAMPLE 2.5: Density as a conversion factor (mass → volume)

What volume of mercury (d = 13.546 g/mL) has a mass of 250.0 g?

Step 1 — Given: m = 250.0 g. Wanted: V in mL.

Step 2 — d = m / V → V = m / d.

\[\text{Step 3 — V =}\ 250.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mL}}{13.546 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 18.456 \text{mL}.\]

"g" cancels via the inverted density (used to convert mass to volume). Four sig figs throughout.

Step 4 — Sig figs: 4 (250.0 and 13.546 both ≥4 sig figs).

Show solution

18.46 mL.

EXAMPLE 2.6: Density as a conversion factor (volume → mass)

What mass of ethanol (d = 0.789 g/mL) is contained in 250. mL?

Step 1 — Given: V = 250. mL. Wanted: m in g.

Step 2 — m = d × V.

\[\text{Step 3 — m =}\ 250. \cancel{\text{mL}} \times \dfrac{0.789 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mL}}} = 197.25 \text{g}.\]

"mL" cancels via the density factor. Three sig figs from "250." (the trailing decimal makes the zero significant) and "0.789".

Step 4 — Sig figs: 250. has 3, 0.789 has 3 → 3 sig figs.

Show solution

197 g.

2.9 Equivalence Conversion Factors Other Than Density

Concentration and Dosage

Pharmacy and clinical chemistry use mass-per-volume factors. A vial labeled "125 mg/5 mL" tells you that every 5 mL of solution contains 125 mg of drug, so the conversion factors are (125 mg / 5 mL) and (5 mL / 125 mg).

EXAMPLE 2.7: Dosage calculation

A child needs 90.0 mg of an antibiotic. The bottle is labeled 125 mg per 5.00 mL. What volume should be administered?

Step 1 — Given: 90.0 mg. Wanted: mL.

Step 2 — Conversion factor: (5.00 mL / 125 mg).

\[Step 3 — 90.0 \cancel{\text{mg}} \times \dfrac{5.00 \text{mL}}{125 \cancel{\text{mg}}} = 3.60 \text{mL}.\]

"mg" cancels via the dose-concentration factor (125 mg per 5.00 mL inverted). Three sig figs.

Show solution

3.60 mL.

Rate Relationships

"Per" relationships are equivalence factors: a car burns 28.0 mi per gallon → (28.0 mi / 1 gal) or (1 gal / 28.0 mi). A reaction proceeds at 0.045 mol/s → (0.045 mol / 1 s).

Cost Relationships

"$3.49 per pound" is a conversion factor exactly like 12 in/ft. The unit "$" cancels by division just like grams or moles.

2.10 Percentage and Percent Error

Percentage is parts per hundred. "45 percent of the alloy is copper" means 45 g of copper per 100 g of alloy, so the conversion factor is (45 g Cu / 100 g alloy) — exactly the kind of factor you have already been using.

Percent error is a measure of how close a measured value is to a true value:

% error = |measured − true| / true × 100%

Some texts allow signed percent error (positive if measured > true, negative if measured < true) to flag systematic bias; the absolute-value form above is the most common.

EXAMPLE 2.8: Percent error

A student measures the density of an aluminum sample as 2.65 g/mL. The accepted value is 2.70 g/mL. What is the percent error?

Step 1 — % error = |2.65 − 2.70| / 2.70 × 100%.

Step 2 — = 0.05 / 2.70 × 100% = 1.85%.

Step 3 — Sig figs: subtraction yields 0.05 (1 sig fig); divide and multiply (sig fig rule) → 1 sig fig in answer.

Show solution

2% percent error.

2.11 Temperature Scales

Temperature is the average kinetic energy of particles in a sample (we will revisit this microscopic definition in Chapter 10). Three scales appear in introductory chemistry.

  • Fahrenheit (°F). Used in everyday US life. Water freezes at 32 °F and boils at 212 °F. The interval from freezing to boiling is 180 degrees.
  • Celsius (°C). Used in laboratories and most countries. Water freezes at 0 °C and boils at 100 °C. The interval is 100 degrees, so a Celsius degree is 9/5 of a Fahrenheit degree (and a Fahrenheit degree is 5/9 of a Celsius degree).
  • Kelvin (K). The SI base unit for temperature. The size of one kelvin equals one Celsius degree, but the scale starts at absolute zero (the lowest possible temperature). Note: there is no degree symbol on K. Water freezes at 273.15 K and boils at 373.15 K.

The conversion formulas:

°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9

°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32

K = °C + 273.15

°C = K − 273.15

CAUTION: Note that temperature CONVERSIONS use formulas, not multiplication by a single conversion factor. The reason: the three scales do not share a common zero, so you cannot just "cancel units." Differences (intervals) of temperature do convert as factors: a 5 °C interval = a 9 °F interval = a 5 K interval.

Significant figures in temperature: zero on the Celsius and Kelvin scales is a defined reference, not a measured value, so the digits before the decimal point in the constant 273.15 are exact. The result of a conversion has the same number of decimal places as the input.

EXAMPLE 2.9: Fahrenheit to Celsius and Kelvin

A patient has a fever of 102.3 °F. Convert to °C and K.

Step 1 — °C = (102.3 − 32) × 5/9 = 70.3 × 5/9 = 39.06 °C.

Step 2 — K = 39.06 + 273.15 = 312.21 K.

Step 3 — Sig figs / decimal places: 102.3 has 1 decimal place; 32 is exact in this formula. So 70.3 has 1 decimal place. After dividing by 9 and multiplying by 5, the answer has 1 decimal place: 39.1 °C. K = 39.1 + 273.15 = 312.25 K → 312.3 K.

ANSWERS: 39.1 °C and 312.3 K.

\[3.50 \cancel{\text{km}} \times \dfrac{10^{3} \text{m}}{1 \cancel{\text{km}}} = 3.50 \times 10^{3} \text{m}\]

\[3.50 \cancel{\text{km}} \times \dfrac{10^{3} \cancel{\text{m}}}{1 \cancel{\text{km}}} \times \dfrac{100 \text{cm}}{1 \cancel{\text{m}}} = 3.50 \times 10^{5} \text{cm}\]

\[3.50 \cancel{\text{km}} \times \dfrac{10^{3} \cancel{\text{m}}}{1 \cancel{\text{km}}} \times \dfrac{1000 \text{mm}}{1 \cancel{\text{m}}} = 3.50 \times 10^{6} \text{mm}\]

In each chain, "km" cancels against the conversion factor, and (for cm and mm) the intermediate "m" cancels too. Three sig figs from "3.50 km" carry through; the metric conversion factors are exact.

2. Convert 0.025 g to

  1. mg,
  2. µg,
  3. kg.
Show solution

\[0.025 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1000 \text{mg}}{1 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 25 \text{mg}\]

\[0.025 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{10^{6} \text{µg}}{1 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 2.5 \times 10^{4} \text{µg}\]

\[0.025 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{kg}}{1000 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 2.5 \times 10^{-5} \text{kg}\]

"g" cancels in each chain. Two sig figs from "0.025 g" are preserved (leading zeros are not significant).

3. Convert 750 mL to L, dL, and µL.

Show solution

\[750 \cancel{\text{mL}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{L}}{1000 \cancel{\text{mL}}} = 0.750 \text{L}\]

\[750 \cancel{\text{mL}} \times \dfrac{1 \cancel{\text{L}}}{1000 \cancel{\text{mL}}} \times \dfrac{10 \text{dL}}{1 \cancel{\text{L}}} = 7.50 \text{dL}\]

\[750 \cancel{\text{mL}} \times \dfrac{1000 \text{µL}}{1 \cancel{\text{mL}}} = 7.50 \times 10^{5} \text{µL}\]

"mL" cancels in each chain (and "L" cancels in the dL chain). Three sig figs are kept; the trailing zero in "750" is taken as significant from context.

4. Express the diameter of a hydrogen atom (~0.1 nm) in m and pm.

Show solution

\[0.1 \cancel{\text{nm}} \times \dfrac{10^{-9} \text{m}}{1 \cancel{\text{nm}}} = 1 \times 10^{-10} \text{m}\]

\[0.1 \cancel{\text{nm}} \times \dfrac{1000 \text{pm}}{1 \cancel{\text{nm}}} = 100 \text{pm}\]

"nm" cancels in both chains. "0.1 nm" carries 1 sig fig, so the answers are reported as 1 × 10⁻¹⁰ m and 100 pm (1 sig fig).

Show solution

\[250 \cancel{\text{µg}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mg}}{1000 \cancel{\text{µg}}} = 0.250 \text{mg}\]

"µg" cancels, leaving "mg". Three sig figs from "250" carry through (the conversion 1 mg = 1000 µg is exact).

"ft" cancels. The relationship 1 mi = 5280 ft is exact, so the result has unlimited sig figs; written as 1.000 mi by convention.

1. A metal cube of edge 2.50 cm has mass 78.4 g. Compute density.

2. What mass of mercury (ρ = 13.6 g/mL) fills a 25.0 mL flask?

Show solution

\[\text{m} = ρ \times V = \dfrac{13.6 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mL}}} \times 25.0 \cancel{\text{mL}} = 340. \text{g}\]

3. A 50.0 g sample of an unknown liquid has volume 63.3 mL. Identify it from: water (1.00), ethanol (0.789), acetone (0.791).

Show solution

Density 0.790 g/mL matches ethanol (0.789) or acetone (0.791) within reading; further testing (e.g., boiling point) needed to distinguish. Three sig figs.

4. A piece of aluminum (ρ = 2.70 g/cm³) has mass 8.10 g. What is its volume?

Show solution

\[V = \text{m} / ρ = 8.10 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{cm}^{3}}{2.70 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 3.00 \text{cm}^{3}\]

"g" cancels, leaving "cm³". Three sig figs throughout.

5. Will a piece of iron (ρ = 7.87 g/cm³) sink or float in mercury (ρ = 13.6 g/cm³)?

Show solution

Iron floats on mercury. Iron's density (7.87 g/cm³) is less than mercury's (13.6 g/cm³), so a piece of iron displaces a volume of mercury that weighs more than the iron itself — buoyancy supports it. (This is why iron cannonballs were stored on a dish of mercury in 18th-century European armouries to keep them rust-free.) Whether an object sinks or floats in a liquid is determined by comparing densities, not masses. ρ_object < ρ_liquid → floats; ρ_object > ρ_liquid → sinks.

Concepts to Remember

  1. SI is built on seven base units (Table 2.1). Every other unit is derived.
  2. Metric prefixes multiply the base unit by a power of ten; learn pico- (10⁻¹²) through giga- (10⁹).
  3. 1 mL ≡ 1 cm³ exactly. The L is defined as 1 dm³.
  4. Mass is matter; weight is force. They are not the same.
  5. Conversion factors are fractions whose value is exactly 1; multiplying by them changes units, never the underlying quantity.
  6. Dimensional analysis is the systematic chaining of conversion factors; if the units come out wrong, the calculation is wrong.
  7. Density is mass per unit volume; it is intensive and unique to a substance under fixed conditions.
  8. Density acts as a conversion factor between mass and volume.
  9. Percent is a ratio per hundred; it is itself a conversion factor.
  10. Percent error compares a measurement to an accepted value: |measured − true|/true × 100%.
  11. Temperature scales need formulas, not factors, because they have different zero points.
  12. Kelvin starts at absolute zero and uses no degree symbol; K = °C + 273.15.

Key Terms Glossary

Absolute zero (2.11) — The lowest possible temperature, 0 K (−273.15 °C); the point at which particle motion is at its minimum.

Celsius scale (2.11) — A temperature scale with water freezing at 0 °C and boiling at 100 °C at 1 atm.

Conversion factor (2.6) — A fraction whose numerator and denominator describe the same amount in different units; mathematically equal to 1.

Density (2.8) — Mass per unit volume of a sample; an intensive property used to identify substances and convert between mass and volume.

Dimensional analysis (2.7) — A systematic problem-solving method in which units are tracked and cancelled through a chain of conversion factors.

Equivalence factor (2.9) — A conversion factor built from a non-metric and non-density relationship such as concentration, dosage, rate, or cost.

Fahrenheit scale (2.11) — A temperature scale with water freezing at 32 °F and boiling at 212 °F.

Intensive property (2.8) — A property that does not depend on the amount of sample (density, temperature, melting point).

Kelvin scale (2.11) — The SI base scale of temperature with absolute zero at 0 K and the same degree size as Celsius.

Metric prefix (2.1) — A modifier (kilo-, milli-, micro-, etc.) attached to a base unit to multiply it by a power of ten.

Percent error (2.10) — A measure of accuracy: |measured − true| / true × 100%.

Percentage (2.10) — Parts per hundred; a ratio that itself acts as a conversion factor.

SI units (2.1) — The Système International d'Unités, the worldwide standard system based on seven base units.

Common Student Difficulties

  • Students treat unit cancellation as cosmetic. Force them to write every factor as a fraction with units explicitly above and below.
  • Multi-step conversions overwhelm. Have them sketch the unit chain (km → m → cm) before writing numbers.
  • Students forget that mL ≡ cm³ — pause specifically to confirm this in lecture.
  • Temperature: students apply factor-label methods to Fahrenheit↔Celsius and lose the +32. Emphasize: temperature CONVERSIONS need formulas, but temperature INTERVALS use simple ratios.
  • Sig figs and percentage: students forget that the 100 in the percent definition is exact. They round too early. Encourage carrying one extra digit and rounding at the end.

Recommended In-Class Activities

  • Density lab: students measure mass and volume of unknown metal cylinders and identify metal from a class table. Reinforces both density and dimensional analysis.
  • Dosage stations: small problem sets with realistic medical scenarios (pediatric dosing, IV drip rates). Connect chemistry to allied health programs.
  • Unit-chain race: each team is given 5 challenging multi-step conversions; first team to set up the chains correctly (without computing) wins.

Lab Connections

  • Lab 1 (recommended): Density of regular and irregular solids; reinforces water displacement, sig figs, and density as identifier.
  • Lab 2: Calibration of glassware (graduated cylinder vs. volumetric flask) — reinforces precision differences across instruments.

Assessment Suggestions

  • Five-question short quiz at end of week 1 of this chapter: focus on metric prefix interconversions and mL/cm³.
  • Hour exam at end of chapter: 10 calculation problems covering dimensional analysis, density, dosage, percent error, temperature.
