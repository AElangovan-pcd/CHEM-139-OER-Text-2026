Chapter 7: Chemical Nomenclature

CHEM 139 — Open Educational Resource (2026 ed.) · CC BY 4.0

Chapter Opener: A Common Language

In the 1700s a chemist who wanted to write up an experiment had to decide whether to call sulfuric acid "oil of vitriol," call iron(III) chloride "butter of mars," or call sodium carbonate "soda ash." The names were colorful, often poetic, and almost completely useless to anyone trying to repeat the work. By the early 1900s, chemists had agreed on a systematic naming scheme — one in which the name of a compound tells you exactly which elements are in it and in what ratio.

This chapter teaches you that scheme. It is not glamorous material; it is the alphabet of chemistry, and you cannot do the rest of the course without it. By the end of the chapter you should be able to take a name like "calcium hydrogen phosphate" and write down Ca(HPO₄), or take a formula like Fe₂(SO₄)₃ and produce the name "iron(III) sulfate."

Why this matters: Names are infrastructure. Once you can convert "calcium hydrogen phosphate" to Ca(HPO₄)₂ and back, you can read a chemical-stockroom shelf, a safety data sheet, a pharmaceutical label, an environmental-cleanup report, and the textbook for any chemistry course that follows this one. Every later chapter in this book assumes you can do this on demand: Chapter 8 expects "calcium phosphate" to give you a formula mass, Chapter 9 expects it to give you a balanced equation. Pre-med and pre-engineering students who skim nomenclature pay for it in every later course; budget the time here so the rest of your science career runs on autopilot.

  2. Apply the correct naming convention to each class.

  4. Name and write formulas for compounds containing polyatomic ions.
  5. Name binary molecular compounds using Greek prefixes.

  9. Read pH values qualitatively, identify acidic, neutral, and basic solutions on the 0–14 pH scale, and recognise common indicator colour changes.

7.1 Classification of Compounds for Nomenclature Purposes

Different classes of compounds use different naming rules. Before you can name a compound you must decide which class it belongs to. We use four classes in this chapter:

  • Binary ionic compounds. Two elements, one of which is a metal cation and the other a nonmetal anion. Subdivided into Type I (fixed-charge metal) and Type II (variable-charge metal).
  • Ionic compounds containing polyatomic ions. More than two elements, with one or more polyatomic ions present.
  • Binary molecular compounds. Two nonmetals (or two metalloids, or one nonmetal and a metalloid) covalently bonded.
  • Acids. Compounds beginning with H, when dissolved in water, that release H⁺ ions. Subdivided into binary acids (hydroacids: H + one nonmetal) and oxyacids (H + a polyatomic ion containing oxygen).

7.2 Types of Binary Ionic Compounds

Binary ionic compounds come in two types based on whether the metal cation has a fixed or variable charge.

  • Type I (fixed-charge). Cation has only one common charge. Includes Group 1 (always +1), Group 2 (always +2), Al (always +3), and a few transition metals fixed in their common ions: Zn (+2), Cd (+2), Ag (+1).
  • Type II (variable-charge). Cation has more than one common charge. Most transition metals fall here. Examples: Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺, Cu⁺/Cu²⁺, Sn²⁺/Sn⁴⁺, Pb²⁺/Pb⁴⁺.

7.3 Nomenclature for Binary Ionic Compounds

Type I — Fixed-Charge Cations

Naming rule: cation name (the metal's English name, unchanged) + anion root with -ide suffix. Example: NaCl = "sodium chloride." CaO = "calcium oxide." Mg₃N₂ = "magnesium nitride" (note the subscripts are not part of the name — charge balance does that work).

Common -ide endings:

  • F → fluoride; Cl → chloride; Br → bromide; I → iodide
  • O → oxide; S → sulfide; Se → selenide; N → nitride; P → phosphide; H → hydride

Type II — Variable-Charge Cations (Stock System)

Naming rule: cation name + Roman numeral in parentheses (= the charge of the metal in this compound) + anion root with -ide suffix. The Roman numeral is essential because the same elements can form more than one compound. FeCl₂ is "iron(II) chloride"; FeCl₃ is "iron(III) chloride."

EXAMPLE 7.1: Naming a Type II binary ionic compound

Name CuS.

Step 1 — Determine the anion charge: S is in Group 16, so S²⁻.

Step 2 — Determine the cation charge by balance: Cu must be +2 to balance one S²⁻.

Step 3 — Name: copper(II) sulfide.

Show solution

copper(II) sulfide.

EXAMPLE 7.2: From name to formula (Type II)

Write the formula for tin(IV) oxide.

Step 1 — Tin(IV) means Sn⁴⁺. Oxide is O²⁻.

Step 2 — Crisscross: Sn₂O₄.

Step 3 — Reduce: SnO₂.

Show solution

SnO₂.

7.4 Chemical Formulas and Names for Polyatomic-Ion Compounds

To write a formula for an ionic compound with a polyatomic ion, treat the polyatomic ion as a unit (consult Table 6.2 for charges) and balance charges. If more than one polyatomic ion is needed, enclose it in parentheses with the count as a subscript.

7.5 Nomenclature for Ionic Compounds Containing Polyatomic Ions

Naming rule: cation name (with Roman numeral if Type II) + polyatomic-ion name (no -ide suffix; use the ion's own name from Table 6.2). Examples: NaNO₃ = "sodium nitrate." K₂SO₄ = "potassium sulfate." Fe₂(SO₄)₃ = "iron(III) sulfate." (NH₄)₃PO₄ = "ammonium phosphate."

EXAMPLE 7.3: Compound with two polyatomic ions

Write the formula for ammonium dichromate.

Step 1 — Ammonium = NH₄⁺. Dichromate = Cr₂O₇²⁻.

Step 2 — Two NH₄⁺ are needed to balance one Cr₂O₇²⁻.

Step 3 — Formula: (NH₄)₂Cr₂O₇.

Show solution

(NH₄)₂Cr₂O₇.

7.7 Nomenclature for Acids

Acids are compounds that release H⁺ ions when dissolved in water. We will revisit the chemistry of acids later; for now we just need their names. Two patterns matter.

A binary acid has the general formula H + nonmetal — in aqueous solution. Naming rule: hydro- + nonmetal root + -ic + acid. The (aq) label distinguishes the acidic solution from the molecular gas (HCl gas vs. HCl aqueous = hydrochloric acid).

EXAMPLE 7.5: Decision-tree application

(a) Mg(OH)₂ — Starts with non-H → has a metal (Mg) → fixed charge → contains polyatomic ion (OH⁻) → "magnesium hydroxide."

(b) FeCl₃ — non-H start → metal (Fe) → variable charge → no polyatomic → -ide. Cation: each Cl is −1, total −3, so Fe = +3. Name: "iron(III) chloride."

(c) CO₂ — non-H start → no metal (C and O are nonmetals) → binary molecular → use Greek prefixes. Name: "carbon dioxide."

(d) HClO₂ (aq) — H start → aqueous → contains O → oxyacid → ClO₂⁻ is chlorite, so HClO₂ is "chlorous acid."

(e) HBr (aq) — H start → aqueous → no O → binary acid → "hydrobromic acid."

7.9 Properties of Common Acids and Bases

Naming acids (Section 7.7) is only the first step. To use an acid or base intelligently — in the lab, in cooking, or in everyday safety decisions — you also need to know how they behave. This section is a tour of the general properties shared by acids and by bases, and of the strong-vs-weak distinction that determines just how aggressively a given acid or base reacts.

General Properties of Acids

Aqueous solutions of acids share a recognisable set of behaviours:

• They taste sour (think lemon juice, vinegar, coffee). NEVER taste a laboratory chemical, but the connection between acidity and sourness is why citrus and tomatoes register as sharp on the tongue.

• They turn blue litmus paper red — the classic indicator test. Phenolphthalein is colourless in acid; methyl orange turns red.

• They conduct electricity in solution because they release ions (acids are electrolytes).

• They react with active metals (Zn, Mg, Fe, Al) to evolve hydrogen gas: 2 HCl(aq) + Zn(s) → ZnCl₂(aq) + H₂(g) ↑.

• They react with carbonates and bicarbonates to evolve CO₂ gas: 2 HCl(aq) + Na₂CO₃(s) → 2 NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g) ↑. (This is the antacid + stomach reaction and the baking-soda + vinegar volcano.)

• They neutralise bases to give a salt and water (Section 9.6.7).

What unifies these behaviours is the presence of H⁺ ions (more precisely, hydronium ions H₃O⁺) in solution. The Arrhenius definition of an acid is a substance that releases H⁺ when dissolved in water:

HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

General Properties of Bases

Aqueous solutions of bases share a different but equally recognisable set of behaviours:

• They taste bitter (soap, baking soda, unsweetened cocoa).

• They feel slippery between the fingers — the slipperiness of soap and the slimy feel of drain cleaner are the same chemistry.

• They turn red litmus paper blue. Phenolphthalein turns bright pink in base.

• They conduct electricity (bases are electrolytes).

• They neutralise acids to give a salt and water.

What unifies these behaviours is the presence of OH⁻ (hydroxide) ions in solution. The Arrhenius definition of a base is a substance that releases OH⁻ when dissolved in water:

NaOH(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

Strong vs. Weak Acids

The distinction between strong and weak acids is about the EXTENT of ionisation in water, not the concentration. A strong acid ionises essentially 100% — every HCl molecule put into water releases its proton. A weak acid ionises only partially — most of the molecules stay intact, and only a small fraction donate H⁺ at any moment.

There are only six common strong acids — memorise them, because everything else at this level is weak:

Hydrochloric acid HCl, hydrobromic acid HBr, hydroiodic acid HI, nitric acid HNO₃, sulfuric acid H₂SO₄ (first ionisation only), perchloric acid HClO₄.

Common weak acids: hydrofluoric acid HF, acetic acid CH₃COOH (vinegar), carbonic acid H₂CO₃ (carbonated beverages), phosphoric acid H₃PO₄ (cola), citric acid (citrus), and most carboxylic acids you will meet in organic chemistry.

Strong vs. Weak Bases

Strong bases are the soluble Group 1 and heavy Group 2 hydroxides, which dissociate essentially 100% in water:

LiOH, NaOH, KOH, RbOH, CsOH (Group 1); Ca(OH)₂, Sr(OH)₂, Ba(OH)₂ (heavy Group 2).

The most common weak base is aqueous ammonia (NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻); only a small fraction of the dissolved NH₃ picks up a proton from water at any given moment. Amines (RNH₂) you will meet in organic chemistry are weak bases by the same logic.

Common Acids and Bases in Everyday Life

Acids you handle every day: stomach acid (HCl, ~0.10 M), vinegar (5% acetic acid), citrus and tomato (citric, ascorbic), carbonated beverages (carbonic + phosphoric), car-battery acid (H₂SO₄, ~5 M), aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid).

Bases you handle every day: baking soda (NaHCO₃, mildly basic), antacids (CaCO₃, Mg(OH)₂, Al(OH)₃), soap and detergents, household ammonia (NH₃, ~10%), drain cleaner (NaOH, often >10%), cement and plaster (Ca(OH)₂).

EXAMPLE 7.9: Classifying acids as strong or weak

Classify each as a strong or weak acid: (a) HCl, (b) HF, (c) H₂SO₄, (d) CH₃COOH, (e) H₃PO₄.

Step 1 — Recall the six common strong acids: HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄.

Step 2 — Anything not on that list is weak at this level.

Step 3 — Apply.

(a) HCl — strong (on the list).

(b) HF — weak (NOT on the list, even though all the other binary halogen acids are strong).

(c) H₂SO₄ — strong (on the list, first ionisation; second ionisation H SO₄⁻ → H⁺ + SO₄²⁻ is actually weak, but at prep-chem level treat H₂SO₄ as strong).

(d) CH₃COOH (acetic acid, vinegar) — weak.

(e) H₃PO₄ — weak.

HF is the trap most students fall into. Bond strength makes the H–F bond hard to break in water, so HF ionises far less than HCl despite the family resemblance.

7.10 The pH Scale

The strength of an acidic or basic solution is reported on a single number line called the pH scale, running from 0 (most acidic) through 7 (neutral) to 14 (most basic). The underlying mathematics is logarithmic (pH = −log[H⁺]) and a full quantitative treatment belongs to general chemistry. At the prep-chem level, you need to read pH values qualitatively, identify common substances by their pH, and recognise indicators.

Reading pH Values

• pH < 7: acidic. The lower the number, the more acidic. pH 1 is highly acidic; pH 6 is just slightly acidic.

• pH = 7: neutral (pure water at 25 °C).

• pH > 7: basic. The higher the number, the more basic. pH 8 is just slightly basic; pH 14 is highly basic.

Each pH unit represents a TENFOLD change in acidity. A solution at pH 3 is ten times more acidic than one at pH 4, and one hundred times more acidic than one at pH 5. This logarithmic compression is why a small change in pH can have a disproportionately large effect (lake acidification, blood-pH disturbances, food spoilage).

Approximate pH of Common Substances

Battery acid: ~0.5; stomach acid: ~1.5–3.5; lemon juice: ~2; vinegar: ~3; orange juice: ~3.5; coffee: ~5; rainwater: ~5.6; milk: ~6.5; pure water: 7.0; blood: 7.4; sea water: ~8.1; baking soda solution: ~9; household ammonia: ~11; bleach: ~12.5; oven cleaner / lye: ~13.5.

The narrow range of human blood pH (7.35–7.45) is held there by buffer chemistry; deviations of even 0.1 pH unit are medically significant.

Indicators

An indicator is a dye whose colour changes in a defined pH range. Three you will meet repeatedly:

• Litmus: red in acid (pH < 5), blue in base (pH > 8).

• Phenolphthalein: colourless below pH 8.3, pink above.

• Methyl orange: red below pH 3.1, yellow above 4.4.

Universal indicator solution and pH paper are mixtures of several dyes that span the whole 0–14 range, giving a continuous colour scale.

EXAMPLE 7.10: Reading pH values

Classify each solution as strongly acidic, weakly acidic, neutral, weakly basic, or strongly basic.

(a) gastric juice, pH = 1.5

(b) saliva, pH = 6.5

(c) tap water, pH = 7.0

(d) blood, pH = 7.4

(e) household ammonia, pH = 11.0

Step 1 — Apply the rules: < 4 strongly acidic; 4–6 weakly acidic; 7 neutral; 8–10 weakly basic; > 10 strongly basic.

(a) pH 1.5 → strongly acidic.

(b) pH 6.5 → weakly acidic (just barely below 7).

(c) pH 7.0 → neutral.

(d) pH 7.4 → weakly basic.

(e) pH 11.0 → strongly basic.

Now order them by acidity, most acidic to most basic: gastric juice > saliva > tap water > blood > ammonia.

Each pH unit is a 10× change in [H⁺], so gastric juice (pH 1.5) is about 100,000 times more acidic than saliva (pH 6.5), even though both numbers are 'less than 7'.

Concepts to Remember

  1. Different compound classes use different naming rules; classify before naming.
  2. Type I (fixed-charge) ionic: cation name + anion-root + -ide.
  3. Type II (variable-charge) ionic: add a Roman numeral in parentheses for the metal's charge.
  4. Polyatomic-ion compounds: keep the polyatomic ion's own name (no -ide suffix).
  5. Binary molecular: Greek prefixes count atoms; "mono-" is dropped on the first element.
  6. Binary acids (HX, aq): hydro- + nonmetal-root + -ic acid.
  7. Oxyacids: anion -ate → acid -ic; anion -ite → acid -ous.
  8. Charge balance always determines subscripts. The criss-cross trick + reduce gives the correct formula.
  9. Use the decision tree (Figure 7.1) to navigate any unfamiliar compound.

Acids taste sour, turn litmus red, and release H⁺ in water; bases taste bitter, feel slippery, turn litmus blue, and release OH⁻ in water.

There are only six common strong acids — HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄ — and the strong bases are the soluble Group 1 and heavy Group 2 hydroxides. Everything else is weak.

Strength refers to extent of ionisation, NOT to concentration: a 0.001 M HCl solution is dilute but the HCl is still 100% ionised.

The pH scale runs 0 (acidic) through 7 (neutral) to 14 (basic); each unit represents a tenfold change in [H⁺].

Indicators (litmus, phenolphthalein, methyl orange, universal indicator) signal pH ranges by colour change.

Key Terms Glossary

Binary acid (7.7) — An acid composed of hydrogen and one nonmetal in aqueous solution; named hydro- + nonmetal-root + -ic acid (e.g., HCl(aq) = hydrochloric acid).

Binary compound (7.1) — A compound containing exactly two elements.

Greek prefix (7.6) — A numerical prefix (mono-, di-, tri-, …) used in naming binary molecular compounds to indicate the number of atoms of an element.

Hydroacid (7.7) — Synonym for binary acid.

Oxyacid (7.7) — An acid composed of hydrogen, an oxy-anion, and oxygen; named by replacing the anion ending: -ate → -ic acid; -ite → -ous acid.

Stock system (7.3) — A naming convention that uses a Roman numeral in parentheses to specify the charge of a variable-charge metal cation (e.g., iron(III) chloride).

Type I ionic compound (7.2) — Binary ionic compound with a fixed-charge metal (Group 1, 2, Al, Ag, Zn, Cd).

Type II ionic compound (7.2) — Binary ionic compound with a variable-charge metal (most transition metals); requires a Roman numeral in the name.

Arrhenius acid (7.9) — A substance that releases H⁺ ions when dissolved in water.

Arrhenius base (7.9) — A substance that releases OH⁻ ions when dissolved in water.

Indicator (7.10) — A dye that changes colour over a defined pH range; used to estimate the pH of a solution.

pH (7.10) — A logarithmic measure of acidity running from 0 (most acidic) through 7 (neutral) to 14 (most basic); each unit corresponds to a 10× change in [H⁺].

Strong acid (7.9) — An acid that ionises essentially 100% in water; the six common ones are HCl, HBr, HI, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄.

Strong base (7.9) — A base that dissociates essentially 100% in water; the soluble Group 1 hydroxides and the heavy Group 2 hydroxides.

Weak acid (7.9) — An acid that ionises only partially in water; HF, acetic acid, carbonic acid, phosphoric acid, and most carboxylic acids are weak.

Weak base (7.9) — A base that ionises only partially in water; aqueous ammonia (NH₃) and amines are the prototypes.

Common Student Difficulties

  • Students name CuO as "copper oxide" without a Roman numeral. Insist on the Roman numeral for ANY transition metal not in the fixed-charge list.
  • Students confuse "-ate" anions with "-ide" anions. Drill with a list of -ate vs. -ide endings.
  • Students use Greek prefixes for ionic compounds ("dipotassium oxide" — wrong). Greek prefixes belong to MOLECULAR compounds only.
  • Conversely, students forget to use Greek prefixes for binary molecular compounds. Drill with NO, NO₂, N₂O, N₂O₃, N₂O₅.

Recommended In-Class Activities

  • Decision-tree posters: each pair receives a list of formulas and walks through Figure 7.1 to name them, displaying their work on a large poster.
  • Naming relay: each student is given one formula or one name and must produce the other; cards rotate.
  • Polyatomic-ion karaoke: print a song-lyric mnemonic; rote learning supported by the music.

Lab Connections

  • Identification of household products: bleach (NaClO), washing soda (Na₂CO₃), milk of magnesia (Mg(OH)₂), aspirin (HC₉H₇O₄). Students name each and classify.
