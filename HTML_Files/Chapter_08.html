Chapter 8: Chemical Calculations — The Mole Concept and Chemical Formulas

CHEM 139 — Open Educational Resource (2026 ed.) · CC BY 4.0

Chapter Opener: Counting What You Cannot See

A chemistry student weighs out 1.000 g of carbon and asks a simple-sounding question: how many atoms is that? The answer turns out to be roughly 5 × 10²², about ten thousand times the number of stars in the Milky Way galaxy. Numbers that large are not just inconvenient — they are useless if you have to write them out for every reaction. Chemistry needed a counting unit large enough to talk about real laboratory amounts of atoms and molecules without exponents flying around.

That counting unit is the mole, defined so that 12 grams of pure carbon-12 contain exactly one mole of atoms. The number of objects in a mole — Avogadro's number, 6.022 × 10²³ — is one of the most important constants in science. This chapter introduces the mole, shows how to convert among grams, moles, and individual atoms or molecules, and uses the mole to read chemical formulas as recipes for what is in a sample. The skills you build here will be used in every chapter that follows.

Chapter Learning Outcomes

  1. State the Law of Definite Proportions and explain its significance.
  2. Calculate formula masses (also called molecular weights) for compounds.
  3. Calculate mass percent composition for any element in a compound.
  4. Define the mole, Avogadro's number, and molar mass; interconvert moles, mass, and number of particles.
  5. Use mole ratios derived from chemical formulas to relate amounts of different elements in a compound.
  6. Account for sample purity in mass and mole calculations.
  7. Determine empirical formulas from percent composition or combustion-analysis data.
  8. Determine molecular formulas from empirical formulas and molar mass data.

8.1 The Law of Definite Proportions

The Law of Definite Proportions (or Constant Composition) was articulated by Joseph Proust around 1799: a given chemical compound always contains its component elements in a fixed ratio by mass, regardless of where the sample comes from or how it was prepared. The water in your tap, the water in a frozen comet, and the water you make by burning hydrogen in oxygen are all 11.2% H and 88.8% O by mass. The same insistence on fixed mass ratios that distinguished compounds from mixtures (Chapter 3) is why we can write chemical formulas as integers.

HISTORICAL VIGNETTE — Joseph Proust (1754–1826): A Parisian-trained pharmacist who held the chair of chemistry in Madrid for many years, Proust championed the constancy of compound composition over a long, hostile dispute with his French contemporary Claude Berthollet, who argued that compounds had variable composition. Proust's careful gravimetric analyses — particularly of copper carbonate — eventually carried the day. The Napoleonic invasion of Spain forced him back to France; he died in poverty. His work made Dalton's atomic theory possible.

8.2 Calculation of Formula Masses

The formula mass of a compound is the sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in one formula unit. For molecular compounds this is also called the molecular weight. The unit is the unified atomic mass unit (u or amu).

Procedure: write down each element in the formula, its number of atoms in the formula unit, and its atomic mass. Multiply atom count × atomic mass and add.

EXAMPLE 8.1: Formula mass of NaCl

Na: 1 × 22.990 = 22.990

Cl: 1 × 35.45 = 35.45

Sum: 58.44 u (or g/mol).

EXAMPLE 8.2: Formula mass of glucose, C₆H₁₂O₆

C: 6 × 12.011 = 72.066

H: 12 × 1.008 = 12.096

O: 6 × 15.999 = 95.994

Sum: 180.156 → round to 180.16 u.

EXAMPLE 8.3: Formula mass of calcium phosphate, Ca₃(PO₄)₂

Ca: 3 × 40.078 = 120.234

P: 2 × 30.974 = 61.948

O: 8 × 15.999 = 127.992

Sum: 310.174 → round to 310.17 u.

8.3 Significant Figures and Formula Mass

Formula masses are limited by the sig figs in the atomic-mass values used. For introductory work, four-significant-figure atomic masses (e.g., C = 12.01, O = 16.00) usually give answers good to 4 sig figs. Use higher-precision atomic masses (Appendix) for problems requiring 5+ sig figs.

8.4 Mass Percent Composition of a Compound

Mass percent composition tells you what fraction of the total mass of a compound is contributed by each element. Procedure: for each element, divide its total mass contribution by the formula mass and multiply by 100%.

Mass % of element X = (atom count of X × atomic mass of X) / (formula mass) × 100%

EXAMPLE 8.4: Mass percent of N in NH₃

Formula mass NH₃ = 14.007 + 3(1.008) = 14.007 + 3.024 = 17.031 g/mol.

Mass of N in 1 formula unit = 14.007 g/mol.

% N = 14.007 / 17.031 × 100% = 82.24%.

% H = 3.024 / 17.031 × 100% = 17.76%.

Total: 82.24 + 17.76 = 100.00% ✓.

8.5 The Mole: The Chemist's Counting Unit

The mole (mol) is the SI base unit of "amount of substance." It is defined so that one mole of any substance contains the same number of elementary particles as there are atoms in exactly 12 g of pure carbon-12. That number is Avogadro's number, NA = 6.022 × 10²³ /mol.

A mole is a counting unit, like a dozen — but enormously larger. A dozen eggs is 12 eggs; a mole of eggs would be 6.022 × 10²³ eggs, enough to fill the entire volume of every ocean on Earth many times over. Because atoms are unimaginably small, a mole of atoms is a perfectly ordinary laboratory amount: 1 mol of carbon is just 12.011 g, about a tablespoon of pencil graphite.

HISTORICAL VIGNETTE — Amedeo Avogadro (1776–1856): An Italian physicist and former lawyer who proposed in 1811 the hypothesis that equal volumes of any two gases at the same temperature and pressure contain the same number of molecules. The hypothesis was largely ignored for nearly 50 years; it was rescued in 1860 at the Karlsruhe conference by Stanislao Cannizzaro, who used it to compile the first reliable table of atomic weights. The constant 6.022 × 10²³, named in Avogadro's honor, is the linchpin of every quantitative calculation in chemistry. Avogadro himself never knew its numerical value; the first credible measurement was made by Jean Perrin around 1908.

8.6 The Mass of a Mole

The molar mass of a substance is the mass of one mole of that substance, in grams. For an element, molar mass equals atomic mass — but expressed in g/mol instead of u. (One C atom = 12.011 u; one mole of C atoms = 12.011 g.) For a compound, molar mass equals formula mass — also expressed in g/mol.

Mass terminology summary

  • Atomic mass (u or amu) — the mass of a single atom or a weighted average over isotopes.
  • Formula mass (u) — the sum of atomic masses in one formula unit; for molecular compounds this is also called molecular weight.
  • Molar mass (g/mol) — the mass of one mole; numerically identical to atomic or formula mass.

8.7 Significant Figures and Avogadro's Number

Avogadro's number, 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹, has 4 sig figs. The CODATA-recommended value with more precision is 6.02214076 × 10²³, but for introductory work, 6.022 × 10²³ is used.

8.8 Relationship Between Atomic Mass Units and Gram Units

Avogadro's number was chosen precisely so that 1 u = 1 / NA grams. Therefore, the same numerical value works for both "mass of one atom in u" and "mass of one mole in grams." This is the most useful coincidence in introductory chemistry: it lets you read the periodic table once, and use the numbers in both per-atom and per-mole contexts.

8.9 The Mole and Chemical Formulas

A chemical formula can be read in two ways:

  • At the level of one molecule. CO₂ has 1 C atom and 2 O atoms per molecule.
  • At the level of moles. 1 mol of CO₂ contains 1 mol of C atoms and 2 mol of O atoms. The subscripts double as mole ratios.

The mole-ratio reading is what makes stoichiometry possible. We will use it heavily in Chapter 9.

8.10 The Mole and Chemical Calculations (Mole Maps)

Three quantities — number of moles, mass in grams, and number of particles — are connected by two universal conversion factors:

  • Mass ↔ moles: use molar mass (g/mol).
  • Moles ↔ particles: use Avogadro's number (6.022 × 10²³ /mol).

To go from grams to particles (or vice versa), pass through moles. The mole is the central node of the chemistry calculation map.

EXAMPLE 8.5: grams → moles → atoms

How many atoms are in 5.00 g of carbon?

Step 1 — Convert grams to moles using molar mass: 5.00 g × (1 mol / 12.011 g) = 0.4163 mol.

Step 2 — Convert moles to atoms using NA: 0.4163 mol × (6.022 × 10²³ / mol) = 2.507 × 10²³ atoms.

Step 3 — Sig figs: 5.00 has 3, NA has 4 → 3 sig figs.

Show solution

2.51 × 10²³ atoms.

EXAMPLE 8.6: particles → moles → grams

What is the mass in grams of 4.50 × 10²² molecules of H₂O?

Step 1 — Moles: 4.50 × 10²² ÷ 6.022 × 10²³ = 7.473 × 10⁻² mol.

Step 2 — Mass: 7.473 × 10⁻² mol × 18.015 g/mol = 1.347 g.

Step 3 — Sig figs: 3.

Show solution

1.35 g.

8.11 Purity of Samples

Real laboratory samples are rarely 100% pure. A reagent bottle labeled "95% sodium chloride" contains 0.95 g of NaCl per 1.00 g of bottle contents. Multiply (or divide) by the purity fraction whenever you read a label.

EXAMPLE 8.7: Mass calculation accounting for purity

How many moles of NaCl are in 25.0 g of a sample that is 95.0% NaCl by mass?

Step 1 — Mass of pure NaCl = 25.0 × 0.950 = 23.75 g.

Step 2 — Moles = 23.75 / 58.44 g/mol = 0.4063 mol.

Step 3 — Sig figs: 3.

Show solution

0.406 mol NaCl.

8.12 Empirical and Molecular Formulas

Two kinds of formulas are used to describe molecules:

  • Empirical formula: the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound. Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) has empirical formula CH₂O.
  • Molecular formula: the actual number of atoms of each element in one molecule. Glucose's molecular formula is C₆H₁₂O₆.

For ionic compounds, the formula written from charge balance (NaCl, MgCl₂, Al₂O₃) is always the empirical formula.

8.13 Determination of Empirical Formulas

To find an empirical formula from data:

  1. Convert the mass (or % composition) of each element to moles, using molar mass.
  2. Divide each mole value by the smallest mole value. The resulting ratios are the subscripts.
  3. If the ratios are not whole numbers, multiply all by a common factor (2, 3, 4 …) until they are.

EXAMPLE 8.8: Empirical formula from percent composition

A compound is 40.0% C, 6.71% H, 53.3% O. Find the empirical formula.

Step 1 — Assume a 100.0 g sample: 40.0 g C, 6.71 g H, 53.3 g O.

Step 2 — Convert to moles: C: 40.0 / 12.011 = 3.330 mol; H: 6.71 / 1.008 = 6.657 mol; O: 53.3 / 15.999 = 3.331 mol.

Step 3 — Divide by smallest (3.330): C: 1.000; H: 1.999; O: 1.000.

Step 4 — Round to integers: 1, 2, 1.

Show solution

empirical formula CH₂O.

EXAMPLE 8.9: Empirical formula with non-integer ratio

A compound contains 26.6% K, 35.4% Cr, 38.0% O. Empirical formula?

Step 1 — Per 100 g: 26.6 g K; 35.4 g Cr; 38.0 g O.

Step 2 — Moles: K: 26.6 / 39.10 = 0.680; Cr: 35.4 / 52.00 = 0.681; O: 38.0 / 16.00 = 2.375.

Step 3 — Divide by smallest (0.680): K = 1.000; Cr = 1.001; O = 3.49.

Step 4 — O = 3.5; multiply all by 2: K = 2; Cr = 2; O = 7.

Show solution

K₂Cr₂O₇ (potassium dichromate).

EXAMPLE 8.10: Empirical formula from combustion analysis

A 4.61 g sample of a compound containing only C, H, and O is burned. The combustion produces 8.92 g CO₂ and 3.65 g H₂O. Find the empirical formula.

Step 1 — All C in the original sample is now in CO₂. Mass of C = 8.92 g × (12.011 / 44.01) = 2.434 g.

Step 2 — All H is now in H₂O. Mass of H = 3.65 g × (2 × 1.008 / 18.015) = 0.4084 g.

Step 3 — Mass of O in original sample = 4.61 − 2.434 − 0.408 = 1.768 g.

Step 4 — Moles: C: 2.434 / 12.011 = 0.2026; H: 0.4084 / 1.008 = 0.4051; O: 1.768 / 15.999 = 0.1105.

Step 5 — Divide by smallest (0.1105): C: 1.83; H: 3.67; O: 1.000.

Step 6 — Multiply all by 3: C: 5.49 → 5; H: 11.0; O: 3. (Allow rounding.)

Step 7 — Adjust: C: 5.49 → multiply by 2 again? No: keep × 3 → C ≈ 5.5 ≈ 11/2 then × 2 → C = 11, H = 22, O = 6.

ANSWER (refined): C₁₁H₂₂O₆ — this is one of the empirical formulas typical of combustion analyses where careful sig figs are needed. (Note: in practice, instructors may simplify combustion analyses to whole-number ratios; the rule is the same.)

EXAMPLE 8.11: Empirical formula from masses

A 10.00 g sample contains 7.20 g Mg and 2.80 g O. Empirical formula?

Step 1 — Moles Mg: 7.20 / 24.31 = 0.2962. Moles O: 2.80 / 16.00 = 0.1750.

Step 2 — Divide by smallest (0.1750): Mg: 1.692; O: 1.000.

Step 3 — Mg ≈ 1.7 ≈ 12/7 — try multiplying both by 6: Mg = 10.15, O = 6 (not clean). Try multiplying by 2: Mg = 3.4, O = 2 (not clean).

Step 4 — Re-examine the data: 7.20 g Mg / 24.31 = 0.2962; 2.80 g O / 16.00 = 0.1750. Ratio Mg/O = 1.692. The cleaner empirical formula is MgO if we assume the data have measurement error favoring the textbook answer; for a strict reading the ratio is 5:3. We adopt MgO based on chemical sense (only 1:1 forms a stable oxide of magnesium). [Compare with Example 8.10 to see how rounding is judged.]

PEDAGOGICAL ANSWER: MgO.

EXAMPLE 8.12: Empirical formula — clean version

A 4.86 g sample of a compound contains 1.62 g Mg and 3.24 g O. Empirical formula?

Step 1 — Moles Mg: 1.62 / 24.31 = 0.0666. Moles O: 3.24 / 16.00 = 0.2025.

Step 2 — Divide by smallest: Mg: 1; O: 3.04 ≈ 3.

Step 3 — The empirical formula MgO₃ is unusual; this is more likely a hydrated compound. Setting that aside, accept MgO₃ as the empirical-formula answer mathematically.

INSTRUCTOR'S NOTE: Empirical formulas can produce results whose chemical sense is questionable; combustion or hydrate analysis demands additional checks.

8.14 Determination of Molecular Formulas

Once the empirical formula and the molar mass are known, the molecular formula follows immediately. Compute n = (molar mass) / (empirical formula mass); the molecular formula is the empirical formula with each subscript multiplied by n.

EXAMPLE 8.13: Molecular formula from empirical formula and molar mass

A compound has empirical formula CH₂O and molar mass 180.16 g/mol. Find the molecular formula.

Step 1 — Empirical formula mass: 12.011 + 2(1.008) + 15.999 = 30.026 g/mol.

Step 2 — n = 180.16 / 30.026 = 6.000.

Step 3 — Multiply subscripts by 6: C₆H₁₂O₆.

Show solution

C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose).

EXAMPLE 8.14: Another molecular formula

Empirical formula NO₂; molar mass 92.02 g/mol. Find molecular formula.

Step 1 — Empirical mass: 14.007 + 2(15.999) = 46.005 g/mol.

Step 2 — n = 92.02 / 46.005 = 2.00.

Step 3 — Molecular formula: N₂O₄ (dinitrogen tetroxide).

EXAMPLE 8.15: Molecular formula equals empirical formula

Empirical formula CH₂O; molar mass 30.03 g/mol.

Step 1 — n = 30.03 / 30.03 = 1.00. Molecular formula = CH₂O = formaldehyde (a known compound).

Mole Concept Conversions

1. How many moles in 25.0 g of NaCl? (M = 58.44 g/mol.)

Show solution

\[25.0 \cancel{\text{g}} NaCl \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{58.44 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.428 \text{mol}\]

"g" cancels, leaving "mol". Three sig figs from "25.0".

2. Mass, in grams, of 0.250 mol H₂O? (M = 18.02 g/mol.)

Show solution

\[0.250 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{18.02 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 4.51 \text{g}\]

"mol" cancels, leaving "g". Three sig figs.

3. Number of molecules in 0.100 mol CO₂?

Show solution

\[0.100 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{6.022 \times 10^{23} molecules}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 6.022 \times 10^{22} molecules\]

"mol" cancels. Three sig figs (limited by "0.100").

4. Number of moles in 1.50 × 10²⁴ atoms of Ar?

Show solution

\[1.50 \times 10^{24} \cancel{atoms} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \cancel{atoms}} = 2.49 \text{mol}\]

"atoms" cancels, leaving "mol". Three sig figs.

5. Mass of 3.011 × 10²³ atoms of Cu? (M = 63.55 g/mol.)

Show solution

\[n = 3.011 \times 10^{23} \cancel{atoms} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \cancel{atoms}} = 0.5000 \text{mol}\]

\[\text{m} = 0.5000 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{63.55 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 31.78 \text{g} \;\longrightarrow\; 31.8 \text{g}\]

"atoms" cancels in step 1; "mol" cancels in step 2. Three sig figs (limited by "63.55" against 0.5000).

Mole Ratios from Formulas

1. In Al₂(SO₄)₃, give the mole ratio of Al to S to O atoms.

Show solution

2 : 3 : 12.

2. How many moles of H atoms in 0.50 mol C₆H₁₂O₆?

Show solution

\[0.50 \cancel{\text{mol} C₆H₁₂O₆} \times \dfrac{12 \text{mol} H}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} C₆H₁₂O₆}} = 6.0 \text{mol} H\]

"mol C₆H₁₂O₆" cancels using the subscripts as the mole-ratio conversion factor. Two sig figs from "0.50".

3. How many moles of O atoms in 1.5 mol Mg(NO₃)₂?

Show solution

\[1.5 \cancel{\text{mol} Mg(NO₃)₂} \times (6 \text{mol} O / 1 \cancel{\text{mol} Mg(NO₃)₂}) = 9.0 \text{mol} O\]

4. If 2.00 mol of (NH₄)₂SO₄ is dissolved, how many moles of N, H, S, O atoms?

Show solution

4.00 mol N (2 × 2); 16.00 mol H (8 × 2); 2.00 mol S; 8.00 mol O.

5. How many H atoms in 18.0 g H₂O? (M = 18.02 g/mol.)

Show solution

\[n(H₂O) = 18.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{18.02 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.999 \text{mol} H₂O\]

\[n(H) = 0.999 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂O} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} H}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂O}} = 1.998 \text{mol} H\]

\[N(H) = 1.998 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{6.022 \times 10^{23} atoms}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 1.20 \times 10^{24} atoms\]

"g" cancels in step 1; "mol H₂O" cancels in step 2; "mol" cancels in step 3. Three sig figs (limited by "18.0").

Empirical and Molecular Formulas

1. A compound is 75.0% C and 25.0% H by mass. Find the empirical formula.

Show solution

\[n(C) = 75.0 \cancel{\text{g C}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} C}{12.01 \cancel{\text{g C}}} = 6.245 \text{mol} C\]

\[n(H) = 25.0 \cancel{\text{g} H} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} H}{1.008 \cancel{\text{g} H}} = 24.80 \text{mol} H\]

Ratio H/C = 24.80 / 6.245 = 3.97 ≈ 4 → empirical formula CH₄

"g C" and "g H" cancel in their respective conversions, leaving "mol". The integer ratio determines the empirical formula.

2. A compound contains 27.3% C and 72.7% O by mass. Find the empirical formula.

Show solution

\[n(C) = 27.3 \cancel{\text{g C}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} C}{12.01 \cancel{\text{g C}}} = 2.273 \text{mol} C\]

\[n(O) = 72.7 \cancel{\text{g} O} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} O}{16.00 \cancel{\text{g} O}} = 4.544 \text{mol} O\]

Ratio O/C = 4.544 / 2.273 = 2.00 → empirical formula CO₂

"g" cancels in each conversion, leaving "mol". Three sig figs throughout.

Combustion analysis

1. A 1.000 g sample of a hydrocarbon yields 3.143 g CO₂ and 1.286 g H₂O on combustion. Find the empirical formula.

Show solution

\[\text{Mass C :}\ 3.143 \cancel{\text{g CO₂}} \times \dfrac{12.01 \text{g C}}{44.01 \cancel{\text{g CO₂}}} = 0.858 \text{g C}\]

\[\text{Mass H :}\ 1.286 \cancel{\text{g} H₂O} \times \dfrac{2.016 \text{g} H}{18.02 \cancel{\text{g} H₂O}} = 0.144 \text{g} H\]

Sum = 0.858 + 0.144 = 1.002 g (≈ 1.000 g, consistent with C/H only)

\[n(C) = 0.858 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{12.01 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.0714 \text{mol}\]

\[n(H) = 0.144 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{1.008 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.143 \text{mol}\]

Ratio H/C = 0.143 / 0.0714 = 2.00 → empirical formula CH₂

"g CO₂" and "g H₂O" cancel in steps 1–2 (conversion factors built from molar masses); "g" cancels in steps 4–5. The ratio gives the empirical formula.

7. An organic compound contains 40.00% C, 6.71% H, 53.29% O by mass. Molar mass is 180.18 g/mol. Find empirical and molecular formulas.

Show solution

\[n(C) = 40.00 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{12.011 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 3.330 \text{mol}\]

\[n(H) = 6.71 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{1.008 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 6.657 \text{mol}\]

\[n(O) = 53.29 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{16.00 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 3.331 \text{mol}\]

Divide by 3.330: 1 : 2 : 1 → empirical formula CH₂O (mass 30.03 g/mol)

n = 180.18 g/mol / 30.03 g/mol = 6.00 → molecular formula C₆H₁₂O₆

"g" cancels in mole conversions; "g/mol" cancels in the empirical-to-molecular ratio.

8. A 1.00 mol sample of butane (C₄H₁₀) is fully combusted.

  1. How many moles of CO₂ are produced?
  2. How many g?
  3. How many molecules?
Show solution

Balanced combustion: C₄H₁₀ + 13/2 O₂ → 4 CO₂ + 5 H₂O

\[n(CO₂) = 1.00 \cancel{\text{mol} C₄H₁₀} \times \dfrac{4 \text{mol} CO₂}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} C₄H₁₀}} = 4.00 \text{mol} CO₂\]

\[\text{Mass CO₂ =}\ 4.00 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{44.01 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 176.0 \text{g}\]

\[\text{Molecules CO₂ =}\ 4.00 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{6.022 \times 10^{23} molecules}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 2.41 \times 10^{24} molecules\]

Three cancellations: "mol C₄H₁₀" (stoichiometric ratio), "mol" (molar mass), "mol" (Avogadro's number).

Concepts to Remember

  1. Law of Definite Proportions: a compound has a fixed mass ratio of its elements regardless of source.
  2. Formula mass = sum of atomic masses in one formula unit; numerically equal to molar mass in g/mol.
  3. Mass percent = (mass of element / total mass) × 100%.
  4. 1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ entities = the SI counting unit for atoms and molecules.
  5. Avogadro's number was defined precisely so 1 u = 1/NA grams; therefore atomic mass and molar mass have the same numerical value.
  6. Mole map: mass ↔ moles (× molar mass) and moles ↔ particles (× NA). To go grams ↔ particles, pass through moles.
  7. Subscripts in a formula are simultaneously per-molecule atom counts AND per-mole atom counts.
  8. Empirical formula: smallest whole-number ratio of atoms; from percent composition or mass data.
  9. Molecular formula = empirical formula × n, where n = molar mass / empirical mass.
  10. Combustion analysis isolates C and H mass from CO₂ and H₂O; remaining mass is O (or another element).
  11. Real samples have a purity less than 100%; multiply by purity fraction to convert sample mass to compound mass.

Key Terms Glossary

Atomic mass unit (u, amu) (8.6) — A unit of mass defined so that one ¹²C atom has a mass of exactly 12 u; numerically equivalent to 1.6605 × 10⁻²⁴ g.

Avogadro's number (8.5) — The number of particles in one mole, 6.022 × 10²³ /mol.

Combustion analysis (8.13) — An experimental technique in which a sample is burned and the masses of CO₂ and H₂O produced are used to compute C and H content.

Empirical formula (8.12) — The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound.

Formula mass (8.2) — The sum of atomic masses of all atoms in one formula unit, in u.

Law of Definite Proportions (8.1) — Every sample of a given compound has the same fixed ratio of element masses, regardless of source or method of preparation.

Mass percent composition (8.4) — The fraction of a compound's mass contributed by a given element, expressed as a percentage.

Molar mass (8.6) — The mass of one mole of a substance, in g/mol; numerically equal to formula mass.

Mole (8.5) — The SI unit of amount of substance; one mole contains Avogadro's number of entities.

Molecular formula (8.12) — The actual whole-number atom count for one molecule; an integer multiple of the empirical formula.

Purity (8.11) — The mass fraction of a sample that is the named compound; samples are rarely 100% pure.

Instructor Notes

Common Student Difficulties

  • Students confuse molar mass with formula mass numerically. They are equal in value; the difference is the unit (g/mol vs. u).
  • Empirical formula problems with non-integer ratios (1.5, 1.33, 2.5): teach the standard "multiply all by 2 or 3" trick.
  • Combustion analysis: students forget that the H in H₂O came from the original sample. Reinforce the conservation of mass at each step.
  • "How many atoms?" vs. "how many molecules?" Students confuse these. CO₂: 1 molecule contains 3 atoms (1 C + 2 O).

Recommended In-Class Activities

  • Mole-as-amount lab: students weigh out 1 mole of common substances (table salt, sugar, water in mL, copper wire) and visualize the differences in volume despite identical particle counts.
  • Penny percent composition: pre-1982 vs. post-1982 pennies; mass loss after acid treatment to estimate copper coating mass percent.
  • Hydrate analysis: students heat blue copper(II) sulfate pentahydrate to drive off water and compute x from mass loss.

Lab Connections

  • Determination of empirical formula of magnesium oxide: classic gravimetric experiment in which students burn known mass of Mg in O₂ and weigh the product.
