Chapter 9: Chemical Calculations Involving Chemical Equations

CHEM 139 — Open Educational Resource (2026 ed.) · CC BY 4.0

Chapter Opener: How Much Air Does It Take to Burn a Gallon of Gasoline?

A gallon of gasoline burned in a car engine releases about 80,000 kJ of energy and pushes the car perhaps 30 miles down the road. To do that, the engine pulls in about 11,000 liters of air — more than four bathtubs of air per gallon of fuel. The number is not a guess; it is calculated from the balanced chemical equation for combustion plus the mole concept you learned in Chapter 8. Stoichiometry is the discipline of computing such numbers, and once you can do it for gasoline you can do it for an antibiotic dose, a fertilizer formulation, or a chemical-warfare antidote.

In this chapter we balance chemical equations, classify reactions into a few common families, and use mole ratios from balanced equations to convert any amount of any reactant or product into any amount of any other reactant or product. Two complications make stoichiometry interesting in real labs: yields are usually less than perfect (percent yield) and reactants are usually not in exactly the right ratio (limiting reactant). We close the chapter with both.

9.1 The Law of Conservation of Mass

Antoine Lavoisier showed in the 1770s — by the careful expedient of weighing closed flasks before and after reactions — that mass is conserved in every chemical change. The total mass of the products equals the total mass of the reactants. No atoms are created and none are destroyed; they are only rearranged.

Conservation of mass is the reason every chemical equation must be BALANCED: the same number and kind of atoms must appear on both sides. An unbalanced equation is mathematically inconsistent with conservation of mass and chemically wrong, no matter how plausible the chemistry looks.

HISTORICAL VIGNETTE — Antoine Lavoisier (1743–1794): A French nobleman, tax collector, and chemist who, with his wife and collaborator Marie-Anne Paulze Lavoisier, replaced the qualitative phlogiston theory with quantitative chemistry. He named oxygen and hydrogen, drafted the first systematic chemical nomenclature, and proved conservation of mass in dozens of reactions in sealed flasks. He was guillotined during the Reign of Terror in 1794, shortly after the chief judge dismissed his appeal with the line, "the Republic has no need of scholars." Lavoisier was 50 years old and at the peak of his work.

9.2 Writing Chemical Equations

A chemical equation is the chemist's algebra: reactants on the left, products on the right, an arrow in between. Anatomy of an equation:

CH₄(g) + 2 O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2 H₂O(g)

  • Reactants (left): the substances present at the start (CH₄, O₂).
  • Products (right): the substances present at the end (CO₂, H₂O).
  • Coefficients (the integers in front, e.g., 2 O₂): how many formula units of each species participate.
  • State labels: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous solution.
  • Arrow: "yields"; double arrows ⇌ indicate reversible (equilibrium) reactions.

9.3 Chemical Equation Coefficients

Coefficients are multipliers placed in front of formulas. They mean "this many formula units (or moles) of this substance participates." Coefficients can be changed during balancing; subscripts inside a formula CANNOT, because changing a subscript would change the identity of the substance. "H₂O" and "H₂O₂" are different compounds (water vs. hydrogen peroxide); changing 2 H₂O to H₂O₂ to balance an equation is a chemistry error, not a math error.

9.4 Balancing Chemical Equations

Balancing means choosing coefficients so that every element appears the same number of times on both sides. The standard inspection procedure:

  1. Write the unbalanced equation with correct formulas.
  2. List the elements present on each side and count atoms.
  3. Balance the most complex molecule first, leaving simple species (free elements like O₂, H₂, single atoms) for last.
  4. Adjust coefficients one element at a time. If an element appears in only one species on each side, try that one first.
  5. If you end up with fractional coefficients, multiply through by the smallest integer that clears the fractions.
  6. Check: every element should balance. Reduce coefficients to smallest whole-number ratio.

EXAMPLE 9.1: Balancing methane combustion

Balance: CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O.

Step 1 — Atoms: C: 1 left, 1 right ✓. H: 4 left, 2 right. O: 2 left, 3 right.

Step 2 — Balance H: place 2 in front of H₂O → CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O. Now H = 4 = 4 ✓; O = 2 left, 4 right.

Step 3 — Balance O: 2 O₂ on the left → CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O.

Step 4 — Final check: C: 1=1; H: 4=4; O: 4=4 ✓.

EXAMPLE 9.2: Balancing iron + oxygen

Balance: Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃.

Step 1 — Try 2 Fe + ½ O₂ × 3 = 2 Fe + 3/2 O₂ → Fe₂O₃; multiply through by 2: 4 Fe + 3 O₂ → 2 Fe₂O₃.

Step 2 — Check: Fe: 4=4; O: 6=6. ✓

EXAMPLE 9.3: Balancing combustion of butane

Balance: C₄H₁₀ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O.

Step 1 — C: 4 → put 4 in front of CO₂. C₄H₁₀ + O₂ → 4 CO₂ + H₂O.

Step 2 — H: 10 → put 5 in front of H₂O. C₄H₁₀ + O₂ → 4 CO₂ + 5 H₂O.

Step 3 — O: products have 8 + 5 = 13. Left has 2 per O₂; need 13/2.

Step 4 — Multiply through by 2: 2 C₄H₁₀ + 13 O₂ → 8 CO₂ + 10 H₂O.

Step 5 — Check: C 8=8; H 20=20; O 26=26 ✓.

9.5 Special Symbols Used in Chemical Equations

Beyond reactants, products, coefficients, and the arrow, several additional symbols may appear:

  • (s), (l), (g), (aq). States of matter: solid, liquid, gas, aqueous solution.
  • Δ over the arrow. Indicates that heat is required for the reaction.
  • Catalyst over the arrow. The chemical formula of a catalyst (Pt, MnO₂, etc.) above the arrow indicates that the substance speeds up the reaction without being consumed.
  • ↑ and ↓. Sometimes used to show evolution of a gas (↑) or precipitation of a solid (↓).
  • ⇌. Reversible reaction; the system reaches equilibrium.

9.7 Stoichiometry: Mole-to-Mole Calculations

The mole ratio between any two species in a balanced equation is the ratio of their coefficients. For 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O, the mole ratio H₂ : O₂ : H₂O = 2 : 1 : 2. From any one quantity in moles, you can compute any other quantity in moles by multiplying by the appropriate ratio.

EXAMPLE 9.4: Mole-to-mole

How many moles of O₂ are needed to react with 5.00 mol of H₂?

Step 1 — Balanced equation: 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O.

Step 2 — Mole ratio: 1 mol O₂ / 2 mol H₂.

\[Step 3 — 5.00 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} O₂}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂}} = 2.50 \text{mol} O₂.\]

"mol H₂" cancels via the 2:1 stoichiometric ratio, leaving "mol O₂".

9.8–9.9 Stoichiometry: Mole-to-Mass, Mass-to-Mole, Mass-to-Mass

The general flow of any stoichiometry calculation:

mass A → moles A → moles B → mass B

Step 1 (mass A → moles A): use molar mass of A.

Step 2 (moles A → moles B): use mole ratio from balanced equation.

Step 3 (moles B → mass B): use molar mass of B.

EXAMPLE 9.5: Mole-to-mass

How many grams of H₂O are produced from 0.250 mol H₂ in 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O?

Step 1 — Mole ratio: 2 mol H₂O / 2 mol H₂ = 1.

\[Step 2 — 0.250 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} H₂O}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂}} = 0.250 \text{mol} H₂O.\]

"mol H₂" cancels via the 2:2 stoichiometric ratio (effectively 1:1).

\[\text{Step 3 — Mass =}\ 0.250 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{18.015 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 4.50 \text{g} H₂O.\]

"mol" cancels via the molar mass of water, leaving "g".

EXAMPLE 9.6: Mass-to-mass

What mass of CO₂ is produced from 8.00 g of CH₄ in CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O?

\[\text{Step 1 — Moles CH₄ =}\ 8.00 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{16.04 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.4988 \text{mol} CH₄.\]

"g" cancels via the inverted molar mass of CH₄.

Step 2 — Mole ratio CO₂ / CH₄ = 1/1; moles CO₂ = 0.4988 mol.

\[\text{Step 3 — Mass CO₂ =}\ 0.4988 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{44.01 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 21.95 \text{g}.\]

"mol" cancels via the molar mass of CO₂.

Step 4 — Sig figs: 3.

Show solution

22.0 g CO₂.

EXAMPLE 9.7: Mass-to-mass — limiting reagent absent

How many g of N₂ are required to produce 100.0 g of NH₃ in N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃?

\[\text{Step 1 — Moles NH₃ =}\ 100.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{17.03 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 5.872 \text{mol} NH₃.\]

"g" cancels via the inverted molar mass.

\[\text{Step 2 — n(N₂) =}\ 5.872 \cancel{\text{mol} NH₃} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} N₂}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} NH₃}} = 2.936 \text{mol} N₂.\]

"mol NH₃" cancels via the 1:2 stoichiometric ratio (N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃).

\[\text{Step 3 — Mass N₂ =}\ 2.936 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{28.014 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 82.26 \text{g} \;\longrightarrow\; 82.3 \text{g}.\]

"mol" cancels via the molar mass of N₂.

EXAMPLE 9.8: Mass-to-mass with multiple products

What mass of O₂ is needed to burn 50.0 g of propane (C₃H₈)?

Step 1 — Balanced equation: C₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂O.

\[\text{Step 2 — Moles C₃H₈ =}\ 50.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{44.10 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 1.134 \text{mol}.\]

"g" cancels via the inverted molar mass of propane.

\[\text{Step 3 — n(O₂) =}\ 1.134 \cancel{\text{mol} C₃H₈} \times \dfrac{5 \text{mol} O₂}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} C₃H₈}} = 5.669 \text{mol} O₂.\]

"mol C₃H₈" cancels via the 5:1 stoichiometric ratio.

\[\text{Step 4 — Mass O₂ =}\ 5.669 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{31.998 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 181.4 \text{g}.\]

"mol" cancels via the molar mass of O₂.

Show solution

181 g O₂.

EXAMPLE 9.9: Mass-to-mole

How many moles of H₂O are produced from 5.00 g of H₂ in 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O?

\[\text{Step 1 — Moles H₂ =}\ 5.00 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{2.016 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 2.480 \text{mol} H₂.\]

"g" cancels via the inverted molar mass of H₂.

Step 2 — Mole ratio H₂O / H₂ = 1; moles H₂O = 2.480 mol.

9.10 Yields: Theoretical, Actual, and Percent

In a real laboratory, you almost never recover 100% of the product predicted by stoichiometry. Side reactions, unreacted starting material, mechanical losses (sticking to the flask), and incomplete reactions all reduce the actual yield.

  • Theoretical yield: the maximum mass of product calculated from stoichiometry assuming complete reaction.
  • Actual yield: the mass of product actually recovered in the experiment.
  • Percent yield: (actual / theoretical) × 100%. Always ≤ 100% for legitimate experiments.

EXAMPLE 9.10: Calculating percent yield

When 10.0 g of CH₄ is burned, 24.0 g of CO₂ is collected. Theoretical yield is 27.5 g. What is the percent yield?

% yield = 24.0 / 27.5 × 100% = 87.3%.

9.11 The Limiting Reactant Concept

In recipes, the ingredient you run out of first determines how many cookies you can make. In chemistry, the limiting reactant is the reactant consumed first; once it is gone, the reaction stops, regardless of how much of the other reactant is left over.

To find the limiting reactant: convert each given amount to moles, divide by its coefficient in the balanced equation, and pick the smaller value. The reactant with the smaller (mol/coefficient) value is limiting; the other is in excess.

EXAMPLE 9.11: Identifying the limiting reactant

10.0 g of N₂ and 5.0 g of H₂ are mixed and react: N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃. Which is limiting?

Step 1 — Moles N₂ = 10.0/28.014 = 0.357. Moles H₂ = 5.0/2.016 = 2.48.

Step 2 — Divide by coefficient: N₂: 0.357/1 = 0.357; H₂: 2.48/3 = 0.827.

Step 3 — N₂ has the smaller value → N₂ is limiting; H₂ is in excess.

Show solution

N₂ is limiting.

EXAMPLE 9.12: Theoretical yield from limiting reactant

Continue Example 9.11. How many g of NH₃ are produced?

Step 1 — Use the limiting reactant: 0.357 mol N₂.

\[\text{Step 2 — n(NH₃) =}\ 0.357 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} NH₃}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂}} = 0.714 \text{mol}.\]

"mol N₂" cancels via the 2:1 stoichiometric ratio.

\[\text{Step 3 — Mass NH₃ =}\ 0.714 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{17.031 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 12.16 \text{g}.\]

"mol" cancels via the molar mass of NH₃.

Show solution

12.2 g (3 sig figs).

EXAMPLE 9.13: Limiting reactant with mass-to-mass

20.0 g of H₂ and 100.0 g of O₂ are reacted: 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O.

  1. Identify the limiting reactant.
  2. Compute the mass of H₂O formed.
  3. Compute the mass of excess reactant left over.

Step 1 — Moles H₂ = 20.0/2.016 = 9.92. Moles O₂ = 100.0/32.00 = 3.125.

Step 2 — Divide by coefficient: H₂ 9.92/2 = 4.96; O₂ 3.125/1 = 3.125. → O₂ is limiting.

Step 3 — From 3.125 mol O₂, moles H₂O = 2 × 3.125 = 6.25; mass = 6.25 × 18.015 = 112.6 g.

Step 4 — Excess H₂ used: 2 × 3.125 = 6.25 mol H₂; mass used = 6.25 × 2.016 = 12.6 g; mass left = 20.0 − 12.6 = 7.4 g.

"mol H₂" cancels via the 2:2 stoichiometric ratio. Two sig figs.

\[4.00 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂} \times \dfrac{3 \text{mol} H₂}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂}} = 12.0 \text{mol} H₂\]

"mol N₂" cancels via the 3:1 stoichiometric ratio.

\[n(AlCl₃) = 0.500 \cancel{\text{mol} Al} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} AlCl₃}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} Al}} = 0.500 \text{mol} AlCl₃\]

\[\text{Mass AlCl₃ =}\ 0.500 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{133.34 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 66.67 \text{g}\]

2. For CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂, mass of CO₂ produced from 2.00 mol CaCO₃? (M(CO₂) = 44.01.)

Show solution

\[n(CO₂) = 2.00 \cancel{\text{mol} CaCO₃} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} CO₂}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} CaCO₃}} = 2.00 \text{mol} CO₂\]

\[\text{Mass CO₂ =}\ 2.00 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{44.01 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 88.0 \text{g}\]

"mol CaCO₃" / "mol" cancel through the chain. Three sig figs.

3. For 4 Fe + 3 O₂ → 2 Fe₂O₃, moles of O₂ needed to react with 0.200 mol Fe?

Show solution

\[0.200 \cancel{\text{mol} Fe} \times \dfrac{3 \text{mol} O₂}{4 \cancel{\text{mol} Fe}} = 0.150 \text{mol} O₂\]

"mol Fe" cancels via the 3:4 stoichiometric ratio. Three sig figs.

4. For 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O, mass of water from 8.00 g H₂? (M(H₂) = 2.016, M(H₂O) = 18.02.)

Show solution

\[n(H₂) = 8.00 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{2.016 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 3.97 \text{mol} H₂\]

\[n(H₂O) = 3.97 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} H₂O}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂}} = 3.97 \text{mol} H₂O\]

\[\text{Mass H₂O =}\ 3.97 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{18.02 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 71.5 \text{g}\]

"g" / "mol H₂" / "mol" cancel in turn. The 2:2 ratio is effectively 1:1.

5. For 2 Mg + O₂ → 2 MgO, mass of MgO from 12.0 g Mg? (M(Mg) = 24.31, M(MgO) = 40.30.)

Show solution

\[n(Mg) = 12.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{24.31 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.4937 \text{mol} Mg\]

\[n(MgO) = 0.4937 \cancel{\text{mol} Mg} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} MgO}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} Mg}} = 0.4937 \text{mol} MgO\]

\[\text{Mass MgO =}\ 0.4937 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{40.30 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 19.9 \text{g}\]

Three cancellations across the mass→mol→mol→mass chain. Three sig figs.

6. For C₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂O, mass of CO₂ from 22.0 g C₃H₈? (M(C₃H₈) = 44.10, M(CO₂) = 44.01.)

Show solution

\[n(C₃H₈) = 22.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{44.10 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.499 \text{mol} C₃H₈\]

\[n(CO₂) = 0.499 \cancel{\text{mol} C₃H₈} \times \dfrac{3 \text{mol} CO₂}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} C₃H₈}} = 1.50 \text{mol} CO₂\]

\[\text{Mass CO₂ =}\ 1.50 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{44.01 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 65.9 \text{g}\]

"g" / "mol C₃H₈" / "mol" cancel; the 3:1 ratio comes from C₃H₈ + 5 O₂ → 3 CO₂ + 4 H₂O.

7. For 2 KClO₃ → 2 KCl + 3 O₂, mass of O₂ from 5.00 g KClO₃? (M(KClO₃) = 122.55, M(O₂) = 32.00.)

Show solution

\[n(KClO₃) = 5.00 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{122.55 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.04080 \text{mol} KClO₃\]

\[n(O₂) = 0.04080 \cancel{\text{mol} KClO₃} \times \dfrac{3 \text{mol} O₂}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} KClO₃}} = 0.0612 \text{mol} O₂\]

\[\text{Mass O₂ =}\ 0.0612 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{32.00 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 1.96 \text{g}\]

Three cancellations through the chain. The 3:2 ratio is from 2 KClO₃ → 2 KCl + 3 O₂.

8. For N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃, mass of NH₃ from 28.0 g N₂? (M(N₂) = 28.02, M(NH₃) = 17.03.)

Show solution

\[n(N₂) = 28.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{28.02 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.9993 \text{mol} N₂\]

\[n(NH₃) = 0.9993 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} NH₃}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂}} = 1.999 \text{mol} NH₃\]

\[\text{Mass NH₃ =}\ 1.999 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{17.03 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 34.0 \text{g}\]

"g" / "mol N₂" / "mol" cancel through the Haber-process chain (N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃).

Theoretical, actual, percent yield

1. In a reaction the theoretical yield is 25.0 g and the actual yield is 22.5 g. Compute % yield.

Show solution

\[\% yield = \dfrac{22.5 \cancel{\text{g}} actual}{25.0 \cancel{\text{g}} theoretical} \times 100\% = 90.0\%\]

"g" cancels. Three sig figs.

2. If a reaction has 78.0% yield and theoretical mass 4.50 g, what is the actual yield?

Show solution

Actual yield = 4.50 g theoretical × (78.0 g actual / 100 g theoretical) = 3.51 g

"g theoretical" cancels via the percent-yield fraction.

3. A reaction yielded 14.0 g of product, theoretical = 17.5 g. % yield?

Show solution

\[\% yield = \dfrac{14.0 \cancel{\text{g}} actual}{17.5 \cancel{\text{g}} theoretical} \times 100\% = 80.0\%\]

"g" cancels. Three sig figs.

4. Why is actual yield usually less than theoretical yield?

Show solution

Side reactions, incomplete reaction, mechanical losses (transferring product), and impurities all reduce actual yield below the stoichiometric maximum.

5. For 2 Na + Cl₂ → 2 NaCl, calculate theoretical mass of NaCl from 4.60 g Na (excess Cl₂). M(Na) = 22.99, M(NaCl) = 58.44.

Show solution

\[n(Na) = 4.60 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{22.99 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.2001 \text{mol} Na\]

\[n(NaCl) = 0.2001 \cancel{\text{mol} Na} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} NaCl}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} Na}} = 0.2001 \text{mol} NaCl\]

\[\text{Theoretical mass NaCl =}\ 0.2001 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{58.44 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 11.7 \text{g}\]

Three cancellations across the chain. The 2:2 ratio is effectively 1:1.

Limiting Reactant

1. For N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃, identify the limiting reactant from 1.0 mol N₂ and 4.0 mol H₂.

Show solution

\[\text{Test N₂ as limiting :}\ 1.0 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂} \times \dfrac{3 \text{mol} H₂}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂}} = 3.0 \text{mol} H₂ needed; have 4.0 \text{mol} H₂ — N₂ would consume only 3.0\]

\[\text{Test H₂ as limiting :}\ 4.0 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} N₂}{3 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂}} = 1.33 \text{mol} N₂ needed; have only 1.0 — N₂ runs out first\]

Conclusion: N₂ is the limiting reactant

Each "if-this-were-limiting" test cancels its own moles. The reactant whose required partner exceeds what is available is the limiting one — here, N₂.

2. Using the limiting reactant in the prior problem, how many moles of NH₃ are produced?

Show solution

\[n(NH₃) = 1.0 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} NH₃}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} N₂}} = 2.0 \text{mol} NH₃\]

"mol N₂" cancels via the 2:1 stoichiometric ratio.

3. For 2 Al + 3 Cl₂ → 2 AlCl₃, identify limiting reactant from 0.50 mol Al and 0.50 mol Cl₂.

Show solution

\[\text{Test Al as limiting :}\ 0.50 \cancel{\text{mol} Al} \times \dfrac{3 \text{mol} Cl₂}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} Al}} = 0.75 \text{mol} Cl₂ needed; have only 0.50 — Cl₂ runs out first\]

Conclusion: Cl₂ is the limiting reactant

"mol Al" cancels in the requirement test. Cl₂ is limiting because the chemistry would need more Cl₂ than is available.

4. Using the limiting reactant from the prior problem, mass of AlCl₃ produced? (M = 133.34.)

Show solution

\[n(AlCl₃) = 0.50 \cancel{\text{mol} Cl₂} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} AlCl₃}{3 \cancel{\text{mol} Cl₂}} = 0.333 \text{mol} AlCl₃\]

\[\text{Mass AlCl₃ =}\ 0.333 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{133.34 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 44.4 \text{g}\]

"mol Cl₂" cancels via the 2:3 stoichiometric ratio (from 2 Al + 3 Cl₂ → 2 AlCl₃); "mol" cancels via molar mass.

5. How much excess reactant remains unconsumed (in moles) in the prior problem?

Show solution

\[\text{Al consumed =}\ 0.50 \cancel{\text{mol} Cl₂} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} Al}{3 \cancel{\text{mol} Cl₂}} = 0.333 \text{mol} Al\]

Al leftover = 0.50 mol initial − 0.333 mol consumed = 0.167 mol Al

"mol Cl₂" cancels in the consumption calculation. The leftover Al is the excess after the limiting reactant has been used up.

Multi-Concept Problems

1. Iron rusts: 4 Fe + 3 O₂ → 2 Fe₂O₃.

  1. How many g of Fe₂O₃ form from 10.00 g of Fe (excess O₂)?
  2. If only 12.5 g of Fe₂O₃ are recovered, what is the percent yield?
Show solution

\[n(Fe) = 10.00 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{55.845 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.1791 \text{mol} Fe\]

\[n(Fe₂O₃) = 0.1791 \cancel{\text{mol} Fe} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} Fe₂O₃}{4 \cancel{\text{mol} Fe}} = 0.08953 \text{mol} Fe₂O₃\]

\[\text{Theoretical mass Fe₂O₃ =}\ 0.08953 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{159.69 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 14.30 \text{g}\]

\[\% yield = \dfrac{12.5 \cancel{\text{g}} actual}{14.30 \cancel{\text{g}} theoretical} \times 100\% = 87.4\%\]

Four cancellations: "g" (mass→mol Fe), "mol Fe" (2:4 stoichiometry), "mol" (mol Fe₂O₃→mass), "g" (yield ratio). Four sig figs reduce to 3 by the end.

2. Lithium reacts with water: 2 Li + 2 H₂O → 2 LiOH + H₂. 0.500 g of Li is added to 50.0 g of water. Determine:

  1. Limiting reactant.
  2. Theoretical yield of H₂ in g.
Show solution

\[n(Li) = 0.500 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{6.94 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.0721 \text{mol} Li\]

\[n(H₂O) = 50.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{18.015 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 2.776 \text{mol} H₂O\]

Divide by coefficient: Li 0.0721/2 = 0.03605; H₂O 2.776/2 = 1.388 → Li is limiting

\[n(H₂) = 0.0721 \cancel{\text{mol} Li} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} H₂}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} Li}} = 0.03605 \text{mol} H₂\]

\[\text{Mass H₂ =}\ 0.03605 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{2.016 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 0.0727 \text{g} = 72.7 \text{mg}\]

Multiple cancellations: "g" twice (mass→mol for both reactants), "mol Li" (1:2 ratio), "mol" (mol H₂→mass). Li runs out first because there are far fewer moles of it relative to its coefficient.

3. Photosynthesis (overall): 6 CO₂ + 6 H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6 O₂.

  1. How many g of O₂ are released when 100.0 g of CO₂ is consumed?
  2. How many molecules of glucose form?
Show solution

\[n(CO₂) = 100.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{44.01 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 2.272 \text{mol} CO₂\]

\[n(O₂) = 2.272 \cancel{\text{mol} CO₂} \times \dfrac{6 \text{mol} O₂}{6 \cancel{\text{mol} CO₂}} = 2.272 \text{mol} O₂\]

\[\text{Mass O₂ =}\ 2.272 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{32.00 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 72.7 \text{g}\]

\[n(C₆H₁₂O₆) = 2.272 \cancel{\text{mol} CO₂} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} C₆H₁₂O₆}{6 \cancel{\text{mol} CO₂}} = 0.3787 \text{mol} C₆H₁₂O₆\]

\[\text{Molecules C₆H₁₂O₆ =}\ 0.3787 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{6.022 \times 10^{23} molecules}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 2.281 \times 10^{23} molecules\]

Cancellations chain: "g" → "mol CO₂" (6:6, then 6:1) → "mol" (twice, for mass and Avogadro). Three sig figs throughout.

4. Aspirin synthesis: salicylic acid + acetic anhydride → aspirin + acetic acid (1:1:1:1 mole stoichiometry). 5.00 g of salicylic acid (M = 138.12) reacts to give 4.50 g of aspirin (M = 180.16). Determine:

  1. Theoretical yield of aspirin.
  2. Percent yield.
Show solution

\[n(salicylic acid) = 5.00 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{138.12 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.03620 \text{mol}\]

\[n(aspirin) = 0.03620 \cancel{\text{mol} salicylic acid} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} aspirin}{1 \cancel{\text{mol} salicylic acid}} = 0.03620 \text{mol} aspirin\]

\[\text{Theoretical mass aspirin =}\ 0.03620 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{180.16 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 6.523 \text{g}\]

\[\% yield = \dfrac{4.50 \cancel{\text{g}} actual}{6.523 \cancel{\text{g}} theoretical} \times 100\% = 69.0\%\]

Three cancellation pairs across the chain plus a final "g" cancellation in the percent-yield ratio. The 1:1 mole ratio is the simplest possible stoichiometry.

5. Synthesis of ammonia: N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃. Industrially, 28.0 kg N₂ reacts with 6.00 kg H₂.

  1. Limiting reactant.
  2. Theoretical mass of NH₃.
Show solution

\[n(N₂) = 28.0 \cancel{\text{kg}} \times \dfrac{1000 \cancel{\text{g}}}{1 \cancel{\text{kg}}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{28.014 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 999.5 \text{mol} N₂\]

\[n(H₂) = 6.00 \cancel{\text{kg}} \times \dfrac{1000 \cancel{\text{g}}}{1 \cancel{\text{kg}}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{2.016 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 2976 \text{mol} H₂\]

Divide by coefficient: N₂ 999.5/1 = 999.5; H₂ 2976/3 = 992 → H₂ is limiting (smaller value)

\[n(NH₃) = 2976 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} NH₃}{3 \cancel{\text{mol} H₂}} = 1984 \text{mol} NH₃\]

\[\text{Mass NH₃ =}\ 1984 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{17.031 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} \times \dfrac{1 \cancel{\text{kg}}}{1000 \cancel{\text{g}}} ... wait — Mass = 1984 \times 17.031 = 33780 \text{g} = 33.8 \text{kg}\]

Cancellations: "kg" → "g" → "mol H₂" → "mol". H₂ is the (slight) limiting reagent because it has the smaller mol/coefficient ratio.

6. Decomposition: 2 KClO₃ → 2 KCl + 3 O₂. Heating 25.00 g of KClO₃ produces 9.50 g of O₂.

  1. Theoretical yield of O₂.
  2. Percent yield.
  3. How many g of KCl are produced?
Show solution

\[n(KClO₃) = 25.00 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{122.55 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 0.2040 \text{mol} KClO₃\]

\[n(O₂) = 0.2040 \cancel{\text{mol} KClO₃} \times \dfrac{3 \text{mol} O₂}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} KClO₃}} = 0.3060 \text{mol} O₂\]

\[\text{Theoretical mass O₂ =}\ 0.3060 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{32.00 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 9.79 \text{g}\]

\[\% yield = \dfrac{9.50 \cancel{\text{g}} actual}{9.79 \cancel{\text{g}} theoretical} \times 100\% = 97.0\%\]

\[n(KCl) = 0.2040 \cancel{\text{mol} KClO₃} \times \dfrac{2 \text{mol} KCl}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} KClO₃}} = 0.2040 \text{mol} KCl\]

\[\text{Mass KCl =}\ 0.2040 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{74.55 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 15.21 \text{g}\]

Five cancellation pairs across three sub-questions. Each product (O₂ and KCl) tracks back to the same starting moles of KClO₃.

7. Combustion of methanol: 2 CH₃OH + 3 O₂ → 2 CO₂ + 4 H₂O. Calculate:

  1. How many g of O₂ to burn 64.0 g of CH₃OH?
  2. Total mass of products?
Show solution

\[n(CH₃OH) = 64.0 \cancel{\text{g}} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol}}{32.04 \cancel{\text{g}}} = 1.998 \text{mol} CH₃OH\]

\[n(O₂) = 1.998 \cancel{\text{mol} CH₃OH} \times \dfrac{3 \text{mol} O₂}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} CH₃OH}} = 2.997 \text{mol} O₂\]

\[\text{Mass O₂ =}\ 2.997 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{32.00 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 95.9 \text{g}\]

Mass of products (by conservation of mass) = 64.0 g CH₃OH + 95.9 g O₂ = 159.9 g (= mass CO₂ + mass H₂O)

"g" / "mol CH₃OH" / "mol" cancel in turn. Conservation of mass means total product mass equals total reactant mass — no need to compute CO₂ and H₂O separately.

8. A student adds 50.0 mL of 1.00 M Pb(NO₃)₂ to 50.0 mL of 1.00 M KI. The reaction Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2 KI → PbI₂ + 2 KNO₃ produces a yellow PbI₂ precipitate.

  1. Limiting reactant.
  2. Theoretical yield of PbI₂ in g.
Show solution

\[n(Pb(NO₃)₂) = 50.0 \cancel{\text{mL}} \times \dfrac{1 \cancel{\text{L}}}{1000 \cancel{\text{mL}}} \times \dfrac{1.00 \text{mol}}{1 \cancel{\text{L}}} = 0.0500 \text{mol}\]

\[n(KI) = 50.0 \cancel{\text{mL}} \times \dfrac{1 \cancel{\text{L}}}{1000 \cancel{\text{mL}}} \times \dfrac{1.00 \text{mol}}{1 \cancel{\text{L}}} = 0.0500 \text{mol}\]

Divide by coefficient: Pb(NO₃)₂ 0.0500/1 = 0.0500; KI 0.0500/2 = 0.0250 → KI is limiting

\[n(PbI₂) = 0.0500 \cancel{\text{mol} KI} \times \dfrac{1 \text{mol} PbI₂}{2 \cancel{\text{mol} KI}} = 0.0250 \text{mol} PbI₂\]

\[\text{Mass PbI₂ =}\ 0.0250 \cancel{\text{mol}} \times \dfrac{461.01 \text{g}}{1 \cancel{\text{mol}}} = 11.5 \text{g}\]

Cancellations chain: "mL" → "L" twice (volume→moles via molarity), then "mol KI" (2:1 stoichiometry), then "mol" (mol PbI₂→mass). KI is limiting because there are 2 KI consumed per Pb(NO₃)₂.

Answer Key: 1-B 2-C 3-B 4-D 5-C 6-C 7-B 8-B 9-C 10-D

Answer Key — Worked Rationales

1. B. Conservation of mass requires the SAME number of atoms of each element on both sides — that's what balancing enforces. Total moles can change.

2. C. N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃ balances atoms (2 N, 6 H on each side) with the smallest whole-number coefficients.

3. B. One reactant breaking into two products → decomposition (AB → A + B).

4. D. Two compounds swap partners (Ag goes with Cl, Na goes with NO₃) → double replacement (AB + CD → AD + CB).

5. C. Coefficient ratio H₂O : O₂ = 2 : 1, so the mole ratio is 2/1.

6. C. n(CO₂) = 1.00 mol C₃H₈ × (3 mol CO₂ / 1 mol C₃H₈) = 3.00 mol; mass = 3.00 mol × 44.01 g/mol = 132 g.

7. B. From 2 mol N₂ we'd need 6 mol H₂; only 3 mol H₂ are available → H₂ runs out first → H₂ is limiting.

8. B. % yield = 75 / 100 × 100% = 75%.

9. C. Cu sits below H in the activity series → Cu cannot displace H₂ from acids → no reaction with HCl.

10. D. (aq) means aqueous — dissolved in water.

Concepts to Remember

  1. Mass is conserved in every chemical reaction; balanced equations enforce this.
  2. Coefficients can change during balancing; subscripts inside formulas cannot (changing them changes the substance).
  3. Five reaction classes: synthesis, decomposition, single replacement, double replacement, combustion.
  4. Activity series predicts whether a single-replacement reaction will occur: a more active metal displaces a less active one (and the same logic for halogens).
  5. Mole ratios from a balanced equation are the heart of stoichiometry: coefficient ratio = mole ratio.
  6. Stoichiometry road map: mass A → moles A → moles B → mass B.
  7. Theoretical yield is the maximum predicted by stoichiometry; actual yield is what is measured; percent yield = actual/theoretical × 100% (≤ 100%).
  8. Limiting reactant: divide each reactant's moles by its coefficient and pick the smallest.
  9. After identifying the limiting reactant, all other amounts (products and excess reactants) are computed from it.
  10. States of matter (s, l, g, aq) communicate physical conditions; Δ above the arrow indicates heat is required.

Key Terms Glossary

Activity series (9.6) — A ranking of metals (or halogens) by reactivity; used to predict whether a single-replacement reaction will occur.

Actual yield (9.10) — The mass of product actually recovered from an experimental procedure.

Balanced equation (9.4) — An equation in which the same number of atoms of each element appears on both sides.

Catalyst (9.5) — A substance that speeds up a reaction without being consumed; written above the reaction arrow.

Coefficient (9.3) — An integer (or rational) multiplier in front of a chemical formula in an equation, indicating how many formula units (or moles) participate.

Combustion (9.6) — A reaction of a substance with O₂ that releases heat; for hydrocarbons, products are CO₂ and H₂O.

Conservation of mass (9.1) — The total mass of products in a chemical reaction equals the total mass of reactants.

Decomposition (9.6) — A reaction in which one substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances.

Double replacement (9.6) — A reaction of the form AB + CD → AD + CB; common in precipitation and acid–base reactions.

Limiting reactant (9.11) — The reactant that is consumed first in a reaction, thereby determining the maximum amount of product.

Mole ratio (9.7) — The ratio of two coefficients in a balanced equation; equal to the ratio of moles of one substance to another.

Percent yield (9.10) — (Actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100%; usually less than 100% for real reactions.

Single replacement (9.6) — A reaction of the form A + BC → AC + B.

Stoichiometry (9.7) — The quantitative relationship among reactants and products in a chemical reaction.

Synthesis (9.6) — A reaction in which two or more substances combine to form a single product; A + B → AB.

Theoretical yield (9.10) — The maximum mass of product predicted by stoichiometry assuming complete reaction and no losses.

Instructor Notes

Common Student Difficulties

  • Students try to balance an equation by changing subscripts. Spend the first lecture insisting that only coefficients can change.
  • Students forget to convert mass to moles before applying mole ratios. Use the road map (Figure 9.1) every problem until it is reflexive.
  • Limiting reactant: students compare moles directly, forgetting to divide by the coefficient. Make division by coefficient the first step every time.
  • Students report yields >100%. Explain that this signals an error (impurity, leftover water, weighing mistake) — not a chemistry miracle.

Recommended In-Class Activities

  • Sandwich-stoichiometry: bring bread, lunch meat, cheese; have students balance the "sandwich equation" 2 bread + 1 meat + 1 cheese → 1 sandwich, then identify limiting reactant for various supply ratios.
  • Balance race: timed worksheet of 12 unbalanced equations of varying difficulty.
  • Activity-series prediction: students predict whether 12 single-replacement reactions will occur, then verify with the activity series.

Lab Connections

  • Stoichiometry lab — synthesis of a salt: students react known amount of one reactant with excess of another, recover and weigh the product, compute percent yield.
  • Limiting-reactant lab — copper(II) chloride + aluminum foil: students vary aluminum mass while keeping CuCl₂ constant; mass of Cu produced plateaus when CuCl₂ becomes limiting.
