Chapter Opener: One Substance, Many Faces

Pour a quart of water into a freezer and an hour later you will have ice. Set the same ice on a hot plate and a few minutes later you will have steam. The substance has not changed; the same hydrogen and oxygen atoms are present in all three forms. Yet ice can hold a child up on a frozen pond, water can carry a kayak, and steam can drive a 100-ton turbine. Same matter, three radically different behaviors.

This chapter introduces the language chemists use to describe matter and its changes — the language we will rely on for the rest of the course. We will distinguish physical states (solid, liquid, gas), physical and chemical properties, intensive and extensive properties, physical and chemical changes, pure substances and mixtures, elements and compounds. By the end you will be able to take a sample in front of you and classify it confidently.

Why this matters: The vocabulary in this chapter — element vs compound, mixture vs pure substance, physical vs chemical change, intensive vs extensive — is the working language of every later chapter and every chemistry course that follows. Element symbols, in particular, are the alphabet you will read fluently for the rest of the year: Chapter 7 builds compound names from them, Chapter 8 builds mole calculations from them, and Chapter 9 builds balanced equations from them. Beyond this book, the same classification skill is what a pre-med student uses to read a medication's active ingredients and what a pre-engineering student uses to specify alloys and polymers. Slow down here and the rest of the course will speed up.

After completing this chapter, you should be able to:

  2. Classify properties as physical or chemical, intensive or extensive.

3.1 Chemistry: The Study of Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. Air, water, rock, plastic, blood, smoke — all matter. Light, sound, electric fields, ideas — not matter. The boundary between the two looks obvious in everyday life, but at the edge of physics it gets subtle (light has momentum, after all). For introductory chemistry, the everyday boundary serves us well.

3.2 Physical States of Matter

Matter on Earth almost always appears in one of three physical states (also called phases): solid, liquid, or gas. A fourth state, plasma, dominates the universe at large (the Sun is plasma) but is rare on Earth's surface. Each state is defined by how the particles inside the sample arrange and move.

3.3 Properties of Matter

A property is a characteristic that helps identify or describe a sample. Two crosscutting distinctions matter.

Physical vs. Chemical Properties

A physical property can be observed without changing the chemical identity of the sample (color, density, melting point, boiling point, hardness, conductivity). A chemical property describes how a sample reacts with other substances — it can only be observed by changing the sample's identity (flammability, corrosion, reactivity with acid).

Intensive vs. Extensive Properties

An extensive property depends on how much sample you have (mass, volume, total energy). An intensive property does not (density, melting point, color, concentration). Intensive properties are usually more useful for identifying substances: "this sample has density 19.32 g/mL" pins down the identity (gold) regardless of sample size, while "this sample weighs 47 g" tells you nothing about what it is.

3.4 Changes in Matter

A change in a sample of matter is either a physical change or a chemical change. The distinction parallels the distinction between physical and chemical properties.

  • Physical change. The chemical identity of the substance is preserved. Melting ice, dissolving sugar in water, evaporating perfume, cutting paper, magnetizing a piece of iron.
  • Chemical change (chemical reaction). Atoms rearrange to produce new substances with different properties. Burning gasoline, baking a cake, rusting iron, fermenting sugar to ethanol, digesting food.

Diagnostic clues for a chemical change include: a new color appears, gas bubbles evolve unexpectedly, a precipitate forms, heat or light is released or absorbed, the change is hard to reverse without a second reaction. Any one clue is suggestive; together they are conclusive.

3.5 Pure Substances and Mixtures

Every sample of matter is either a pure substance or a mixture. A pure substance has a single, fixed chemical composition; a mixture is two or more substances physically combined in a ratio that can vary.

3.6 Heterogeneous and Homogeneous Mixtures

A homogeneous mixture has a uniform composition throughout — every drop or every spoonful looks the same. Filtered seawater, sugar dissolved in water, brass (an alloy of copper and zinc), and ordinary air are homogeneous. Another name for a homogeneous mixture is a solution.

A heterogeneous mixture has visibly distinguishable regions of different composition. Granite (you can see the speckles of feldspar, quartz, and mica), oil-and-vinegar salad dressing, a chocolate chip cookie, and ordinary soil are heterogeneous.

REMINDER: The component substances of a mixture retain their own properties. Salt dissolved in water still tastes salty; the water still freezes (at a slightly lower temperature). Mixtures are not formed by chemical reactions and can usually be separated by physical means: filtration, distillation, evaporation, magnetic separation, chromatography.

3.7 Elements and Compounds

An element is a pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. Iron, gold, oxygen, hydrogen, and carbon are elements. There are 118 confirmed elements; 94 of them occur naturally on Earth, the rest only in particle accelerators.

A compound is a pure substance that consists of two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed mass ratio. Water (H₂O) always consists of hydrogen and oxygen in a mass ratio of about 1:8. Sodium chloride (NaCl) always consists of sodium and chlorine in a fixed ratio. Compounds, unlike mixtures, can only be separated into their elements by chemical reactions.

3.8 Discovery and Abundance of the Elements

New elements have been discovered at a roughly steady rate over the last 250 years. Hydrogen was identified by Henry Cavendish in 1766; oxygen and nitrogen in the 1770s; the rare-earth elements over the 1800s; the noble gases by William Ramsay in the 1890s; and the heavier transactinides (atomic numbers 104+) in particle accelerators from the 1960s onward. The most recent additions to the periodic table — nihonium (Nh), moscovium (Mc), tennessine (Ts), and oganesson (Og) — were officially named in 2016.

Element distribution is wildly uneven. The crust, atmosphere, and human body each have a few elements that dominate.

HISTORICAL VIGNETTE — Joseph Priestley (1733–1804): An English clergyman and self-taught chemist, Priestley discovered oxygen in 1774 by heating mercuric oxide and noticing that a candle burned more brightly in the gas given off. He called it "dephlogisticated air," because he interpreted his result through the (now-discarded) phlogiston theory. Priestley's careful experimental notes were later read by Antoine Lavoisier, who renamed the gas oxygène and reinterpreted it correctly. Priestley's career is a useful reminder that data can outlive the theoretical framework in which they were collected.

HISTORICAL VIGNETTE — Jöns Jakob Berzelius (1779–1848): The Swedish chemist Berzelius is responsible for nearly the entire system of one- and two-letter element symbols still used today. He proposed that each element be represented by the first letter of its Latin name, with a second letter added for disambiguation. The Latin origin explains the modern symbols Fe (ferrum) for iron, Pb (plumbum) for lead, Au (aurum) for gold, Ag (argentum) for silver, and Hg (hydrargyrum) for mercury. Berzelius also discovered cerium, selenium, silicon, and thorium, and produced impressively accurate atomic-mass measurements decades before atoms could be observed directly.

Each element has a one- or two-letter symbol. The first letter is always uppercase; the second, if present, is always lowercase. "Co" is cobalt; "CO" is carbon monoxide (carbon and oxygen). Capitalization is not optional.

Most symbols match the English name (H for hydrogen, C for carbon, N for nitrogen). A small set come from Latin or other historical names — they are worth memorizing.

(1) In a liquid, particles are close together; in a gas, they are far apart. (2) In a liquid, particles cannot easily compress (move closer); a gas is highly compressible.

18. Why does oxygen top both lists, despite being a gas at room temperature?

Show solution

19. Carbon makes up only about 0.02% of Earth's crust but ~18% of the human body. What does this tell us about life?

Show solution

Living organisms preferentially incorporate carbon (the backbone of organic molecules) far above its crustal abundance — life is a chemical concentrator of certain elements.

20. Estimate the mass of oxygen (any chemical form) in a 70.0 kg adult.

Show solution

\[\text{Mass O =}\ 70.0 \cancel{\text{kg}} body \times \dfrac{65 \text{kg} O}{100 \cancel{\text{kg}} body} = 45.5 \text{kg} O\]

"kg body" cancels using the percent-by-mass factor as a conversion factor; the result is the mass of oxygen (in any chemical form: water, organic molecules, etc.).

1. List, in order of decreasing mass abundance, the four most abundant elements in Earth’s crust.

Show solution

Show solution

3. Which two elements together account for more than 98% of the mass of the universe?

Show solution

4. The mass abundance of iron in Earth’s crust is about 5%. What mass of iron, in metric tons, is contained in 1.0 × 10⁶ metric tons of crustal rock?

Show solution

\[\text{Mass Fe =}\ 1.0 \times 10^{6} \cancel{tons} crust \times \dfrac{5 tons Fe}{100 \cancel{tons} crust} = 5 \times 10^{4} tons Fe\]

"tons crust" cancels using the 5% mass-abundance factor. Two sig figs (limited by the rough "5%" estimate).

5. Why is oxygen the most abundant element in both Earth’s crust and the human body, despite being unrelated origins?

Show solution

Oxygen forms strong bonds with most metals and nonmetals. In the crust it is locked into silicates and oxides; in the body it is largely the oxygen in water. Oxygen’s chemistry, not a single source, explains its dominance in both.

  1. Physical or chemical change?
  2. The 12.7 g residue does not respond to a magnet. What does mass loss tell us about the gas (mass)?
  3. If the gas is collected and weighed at 7.3 g, does mass total balance? What law is illustrated?
Show solution
  1. Chemical — mass loss with gas evolution implies decomposition into new substances.
  2. Mass of gas = 20.0 − 12.7 = 7.3 g.
  3. 12.7 g (residue) + 7.3 g (gas) = 20.0 g — equal to starting mass. Illustrates the law of conservation of mass.

Concepts to Remember

  1. Matter is anything with mass that occupies space; energy and ideas are not matter.
  2. Matter exists primarily as solid, liquid, or gas; the differences are explained by particle arrangement and motion.
  3. Physical properties are observed without changing identity; chemical properties describe how a substance reacts.
  4. Intensive properties (density, melting point) are independent of sample size; extensive properties (mass, volume) are not.
  5. Physical changes preserve identity; chemical changes (reactions) produce new substances.
  6. Pure substances have a single composition; mixtures have variable composition.
  7. Pure substances are either elements (cannot be broken down chemically) or compounds (two or more elements in fixed ratio).
  8. Mixtures are either homogeneous (uniform throughout, also called solutions) or heterogeneous (visibly different regions).
  9. Element symbols use one capital letter or one capital + one lowercase letter; capitalization conveys identity.
  10. There are 118 confirmed elements; 94 occur naturally on Earth, the rest in particle accelerators.
  11. Oxygen is the most abundant element in both Earth's crust and the human body — but in chemically combined form, not free O₂.
  12. Mass is conserved in any physical or chemical change in a closed system.

Key Terms Glossary

Chemical change (3.4) — A process in which substances are converted into different substances with new chemical compositions and properties.

Chemical property (3.3) — A property that describes how a substance reacts with other substances; observable only through a chemical change.

Compound (3.7) — A pure substance composed of two or more elements chemically combined in a fixed mass ratio.

Element (3.7) — A pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.

Extensive property (3.3) — A property that depends on the amount of sample present (mass, volume, total energy).

Heterogeneous mixture (3.6) — A mixture in which the composition varies visibly from one region to another.

Homogeneous mixture (3.6) — A mixture with a uniform composition throughout; another name for a solution.

Intensive property (3.3) — A property that does not depend on the amount of sample (density, temperature, melting point).

Matter (3.1) — Anything that has mass and takes up space.

Mixture (3.5) — A physical combination of two or more substances in proportions that can vary.

Physical change (3.4) — A process in which the chemical identity of a substance is preserved (melting, dissolving, evaporating).

Physical property (3.3) — A characteristic that can be observed without changing the chemical identity of a sample.

Pure substance (3.5) — Matter with a single, fixed chemical composition; either an element or a compound.

States of matter (3.2) — The three physical forms commonly seen on Earth: solid, liquid, and gas.

Instructor Notes

Show Instructor Notes

Common Student Difficulties

  • Students conflate mixture with compound. Use NaCl (compound, fixed 1:1 atom ratio) vs. saltwater (mixture, variable ratio) repeatedly.
  • Students mistake intensive for chemical and extensive for physical. The two distinctions are independent — mass is physical and extensive; density is physical and intensive; reactivity with HCl is chemical (and intensive).
  • Capitalization of symbols is not optional. Students who write "co" instead of "Co" need to be corrected early.
  • Memorizing the 50 most-common element symbols is a real chore. Distribute it across two weeks; quiz with flashcards or apps.

Recommended In-Class Activities

  • Sample tray: bring in five sealed unknowns (saltwater, sand+iron filings, distilled water, copper wire, granite). Have students classify by inspection, then negotiate disagreements.
  • Property poster: each pair gets one element and produces a poster of three physical and three chemical properties; gallery walk.
  • Element symbol races: timed flashcard drills for the top-50 most common elements (start with the first 20).

Lab Connections

  • Separation of mixtures lab: filter sand from saltwater, evaporate the filtrate, recover dissolved salt. Reinforces homogeneous vs heterogeneous and conservation of mass.
  • Burning magnesium ribbon (with proper safety): observable mass increase as Mg combines with O₂ illustrates chemical change and conservation of mass when the gaseous reactant is accounted for.
