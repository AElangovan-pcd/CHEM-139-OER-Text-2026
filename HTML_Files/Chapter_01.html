Chapter Opener: Why a Chapter About How Science Works Belongs in a Chemistry Book

On a rainy afternoon, a community-college student notices that a glass of iced tea "sweats" while a glass of room-temperature water on the same table does not. A quick search turns up a confident answer on a forum: "That is dew from condensing humidity." A second search turns up a different confident answer: "It is water leaking through tiny pores in the glass." Two answers, both delivered with certainty, only one of them correct. How do we decide?

That deciding-process is what this chapter is about. Chemistry is not a list of facts to memorize; it is a way of asking questions and a discipline for handling the numbers that come back from those questions. In Part A we look at how scientists know what they know — what counts as a fact, a law, a hypothesis, or a theory, and what science cannot decide. In Part B we look at the language scientists use to report their measurements: significant figures, scientific notation, accuracy, and precision. Together these tools turn a casual observation about a sweating glass into a question we can actually answer with experiments and numbers.

Why this matters: Almost every number in this book — a density, a percent yield, a molar mass — is governed by the sig-fig and scientific-notation rules in Part B. Get them right here and they keep paying off in general chemistry, organic chemistry, and any pre-engineering or pre-med lab where a misreported measurement has real consequences. The fact-vs-law-vs-theory literacy from Part A is the same lens you will use to read studies, evaluate competing claims, and design your own experiments throughout your science career. The other nine chapters of this book quietly assume you can do all of this.

Part A — The Science of Chemistry

1.1 Chemistry as a Scientific Discipline

Chemistry is the branch of science that studies matter — the stuff that has mass and takes up space — and the changes matter undergoes. Every object you can touch, taste, or weigh is a chemistry problem in disguise. The salt that flavors your fries, the lithium battery in your phone, the proteins in a vaccine, the asphalt in the road outside your window: all matter, all governed by the same small set of rules that this textbook is going to introduce.

Chemistry is one of the natural sciences, the family of disciplines that try to explain the physical world by careful observation and reasoning. Its closest neighbors are physics (which studies energy and the most fundamental laws governing matter), biology (which studies living matter), geology (which studies the matter of Earth), and astronomy (which studies matter beyond Earth). The boundaries between these fields are softer than the names suggest. Biochemistry sits at the chemistry–biology border. Astrochemistry sits at the chemistry–astronomy border. Materials science is a chemistry–physics–engineering hybrid. Throughout this book we will sometimes step over those borders, because real questions rarely respect academic boundaries.

NOTE: The word "chemistry" descends from the medieval Arabic word al-kīmiyāʾ, the ancestor of the English word "alchemy." Modern chemistry grew out of alchemy in the 17th and 18th centuries when practitioners began insisting on careful measurement and reproducible procedures.

1.2 Scientific Research and Technology

It is useful to separate two related-but-different activities. Scientific research is the systematic investigation of how the natural world works. Technology is the practical application of scientific knowledge to solve a problem or to build a tool. Research asks "what is true?"; technology asks "what can we do with what is true?"

The two feed each other. Research into the structure of DNA in the 1950s eventually enabled the technology of polymerase chain reaction (PCR) testing in the 1980s, which in turn enabled affordable COVID-19 testing in 2020. Technology also feeds back into research: better laboratory instruments allow chemists to measure smaller quantities, faster reactions, and finer details of structure than ever before. Mass spectrometers, magnetic resonance machines, and high-performance computers are all technologies that today drive new research.

This loop has a practical consequence for you as a student: the chemistry you learn this term is not a finished product. It is a snapshot of an ongoing conversation between curiosity and capability.

1.3 The Scope of Chemistry

Chemistry is conventionally divided into five overlapping subfields:

  • Organic chemistry — the study of carbon-containing compounds. Carbon's ability to form long chains and rings underpins all known life and most pharmaceuticals, plastics, and fuels.
  • Inorganic chemistry — the study of compounds that do not feature carbon as the structural backbone, including metals, minerals, and many catalysts.
  • Physical chemistry — the study of the physical principles (energy, motion, equilibrium) that govern chemical change.
  • Analytical chemistry — the study of how to measure what is present in a sample and in what amount.
  • Biochemistry — the study of the chemistry of living systems, from single enzymes to entire organisms.

Modern problems often draw on several of these at once. Designing a new drug, for instance, demands organic chemistry (to synthesize the molecule), biochemistry (to understand how it interacts with a target protein), analytical chemistry (to verify purity), and physical chemistry (to predict stability). The categories are useful labels, not isolated rooms.

1.4 The Scientific Method — Experiments, Observations, and Data

Scientific knowledge is not produced by a single moment of insight; it is produced by a cycle of question, test, and revision. We call that cycle the scientific method. The cycle is usually presented in five stages, but it almost never runs through them in a straight line.

  1. Observation — noticing a pattern or anomaly worth explaining.
  2. Question — formulating a clear, testable question about the observation.
  3. Hypothesis — proposing a tentative explanation that can be tested.
  4. Experiment — designing a procedure that produces data able to support or refute the hypothesis.
  5. Analysis and revision — interpreting the data, refining the hypothesis, and either repeating the cycle or, after enough success, packaging the explanation as a theory.

As the cycle iterates, four kinds of statements come out of it: facts, laws, hypotheses, and theories. Each plays a different role, and confusing them is a common student error.

Scientific Facts

A scientific fact is a single, repeatable observation. "The mercury in this thermometer rose to a height of 28.4 cm when the thermometer was placed in boiling water at sea level" is a fact. Facts are not disputed in the same way that theories are; they are checked by re-doing the measurement. A useful rule is that a fact is a what without yet being a why.

Scientific Laws

A scientific law is a concise summary of a large body of facts. The law of conservation of mass — "in any chemical reaction, the total mass of the products equals the total mass of the reactants" — does not explain why mass is conserved; it just states the pattern. Laws are usually stated as equations or short sentences and almost always carry the name of the chemist or physicist who first articulated them. Laws can be modified or restricted (Newton's law of gravitation, for example, is an excellent approximation but breaks down at the scale of black holes), but they are rarely overturned outright.

Scientific Hypotheses

A scientific hypothesis is a tentative, testable explanation for a fact or pattern. "The reason the iced-tea glass sweats is that water vapor in the warm air condenses on the cold surface of the glass" is a hypothesis. The crucial word is testable. "The glass sweats because tiny invisible spirits exhale onto it" is not a hypothesis, because no experiment can rule it out. A hypothesis must specify what would count as evidence against it.

Scientific Theories

A scientific theory is a well-tested, broad explanation that ties together many facts, laws, and confirmed hypotheses. The atomic theory of matter, the kinetic-molecular theory, the theory of evolution by natural selection, and the theory of plate tectonics are all examples. In ordinary speech, the word "theory" is sometimes used to mean a guess ("my theory is that the cat unplugged the router"). In science the word means almost the opposite: a theory is the most rigorous, comprehensive kind of explanation we have. Theories can be refined as new evidence emerges, but they are not discarded lightly.

Worked Examples — Classifying Statements

EXAMPLE 1.1: Fact, law, hypothesis, or theory?

Statement: "All matter is made of small particles called atoms that combine in fixed ratios to form compounds."

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Identify the scope. The statement describes all matter, not a single observation. So it is broader than a fact.

Step 2 — Ask whether it summarizes data without explanation, or explains why something happens. It explains why compounds form in fixed ratios, by appealing to invisible atoms. So it is more than a law.

Step 3 — Ask whether it has been extensively tested. The atomic model has been confirmed by 200+ years of measurement. So it is more than a hypothesis.

Step 4 — Conclude: the statement is a theory (the atomic theory).

CHECK: Theories explain; laws summarize; facts observe; hypotheses propose. The verb "is made of" is explanatory, consistent with a theory.

EXAMPLE 1.2: Fact, law, hypothesis, or theory?

Statement: "In a sealed flask, the mass of the contents before a chemical reaction equals the mass of the contents after the reaction."

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Determine whether the statement is about a single experiment or a general pattern. It is a general pattern stated for any sealed flask.

Step 2 — Determine whether it explains why the pattern holds, or merely states the pattern. It only states the pattern; it does not explain why mass is conserved (that requires the atomic theory).

Step 3 — Conclude: the statement is a scientific law (the law of conservation of mass).

CHECK: A law summarizes; it does not explain. The statement matches that profile.

EXAMPLE 1.3: Fact, law, hypothesis, or theory?

Statement: "The corrosion on the bridge over Mill Creek is happening faster on the south face than on the north face because the south face is exposed to more direct sunlight."

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Notice the proposed cause-and-effect: "because" links a pattern (more corrosion on the south) to a proposed mechanism (more sunlight).

Step 2 — Ask whether the explanation has been extensively tested. The statement is offered as a tentative explanation for one bridge; no body of confirmation is reported.

Step 3 — Ask whether the statement is testable. It is — a chemist could measure rust thickness on both faces, log sunlight exposure, and check for a correlation.

Step 4 — Conclude: the statement is a hypothesis. With enough supporting data across many bridges, a chemist might eventually generalize it into a law (perhaps relating corrosion rate to UV exposure).

CHECK: The keyword "because" combined with the lack of broad confirmation flags this as a hypothesis rather than a theory.

EXAMPLE 1.4: Fact, law, hypothesis, or theory?

Statement: "When 1.000 g of sodium was added to 1.000 g of chlorine in a sealed flask in lab on March 14, 2026, the temperature of the flask rose by 11.8 °C."

SOLUTION

Step 1 — The statement describes a specific measurement, on a specific day, with specific quantities.

Step 2 — It does not generalize and it does not explain.

Step 3 — Conclude: the statement is a fact.

CHECK: Single observations with named conditions are facts.

1.5 The Limitations of the Scientific Method

The scientific method is powerful, but it is not omnipotent. Three limitations matter for a chemistry student:

  • Reproducibility. A finding is only scientifically meaningful if other researchers, working independently, can reproduce it. A single dramatic result in one lab is the start of an investigation, not its conclusion.
  • Falsifiability. Only claims that could in principle be shown to be wrong belong inside science. "This metal will turn into gold under the right conditions, but I will not specify the conditions" is not falsifiable, and so it is not a scientific claim.
  • Provisional truth. Even our best theories are subject to revision when new data demand it. Newtonian mechanics was the gold standard for centuries; it is now understood as a special case of Einstein's relativity, which itself may someday be folded into a deeper theory.

1.6 Application Limitations for the Methods of Science

Some questions cannot be answered by the methods of science at all. "Was it morally right for the engineer to release the gas mixture into the lake?" "Is this composer's symphony more beautiful than that one?" "What does it mean to live a good life?" These questions matter, but they are questions about values, ethics, and aesthetics. Science can inform our reasoning about them — for example, by quantifying the ecological harm of the gas release — but it cannot decide them. Recognizing the difference between a scientific question and a non-scientific question is one of the most useful habits a citizen of a technology-driven world can develop.

HISTORICAL VIGNETTE: Antoine Lavoisier (1743–1794) is often called the father of modern chemistry. His insistence on careful weighing of reactants and products in sealed flasks — a habit that seems obvious today but was revolutionary in his time — produced the first quantitative confirmation of the law of conservation of mass and ended the centuries-long detour of phlogiston theory. Lavoisier's career also illustrates a sober point about science and society: he was beheaded during the French Revolution despite his scientific stature. The judge who sentenced him is reported to have said, "The Republic has no need of scholars." The mathematician Joseph-Louis Lagrange remarked the next day, "It took them only an instant to cut off that head, and a hundred years may not be enough to produce another like it."

Part B — Numbers from Measurements

1.7 The Importance of Measurement

Chemistry runs on numbers. "The reaction was fast" is a casual sentence; "the reaction was complete in 4.7 ± 0.2 seconds" is a chemical sentence. The transition between the two is the move from observation to measurement, and it is the move that lets one chemist's results be trusted by another chemist on the other side of the world.

Every measurement carries two pieces of information: a numerical value and a unit. The numerical value 4.7 means nothing without the word "seconds." Throughout the rest of this book, you will see units written, never dropped. Dropping units is the single most common source of mistakes in introductory chemistry; it is also the one most easily prevented.

1.8 Exact and Inexact Numbers

Numbers fall into two categories. Exact numbers have a defined value with no uncertainty. There are exactly 12 eggs in a dozen. There are exactly 2.54 cm in 1 inch (this is now a defined relationship). There are exactly 100 cm in 1 m. Counted whole objects (4 students, 7 flasks) are exact.

Inexact numbers come from measurements. They have uncertainty because no measuring instrument is perfect. Reading a graduated cylinder, a balance, or a thermometer always involves some judgment about the last digit. Whenever you weigh something or measure a length, you are producing an inexact number.

CAUTION: It is tempting to call a number like 9.8 m/s² "exact" because it is short and printed in a textbook. It is not. The acceleration due to gravity at Earth's surface is approximately 9.80665 m/s², measured. Defined values (like 1 inch ≡ 2.54 cm) are exact; measured values are not.

1.9 Accuracy, Precision, and Error

Two related-but-distinct concepts describe how good a measurement is.

  • Accuracy is how close a measured value is to the true (or accepted) value. A scale that reads 50.1 g for a calibration weight whose true mass is 50.0 g is highly accurate.
  • Precision is how close repeated measurements are to one another. A scale that reads 47.32, 47.31, and 47.33 g three times in a row is very precise — but if the calibration weight's true mass is 50.0 g, those readings are not accurate.

A measurement can be precise but not accurate (consistent and wrong), accurate but not precise (correct on average but scattered), neither, or both. The famous dartboard image — three darts tightly grouped on the bull's-eye is precise and accurate; three tightly grouped on the rim is precise but inaccurate; three scattered around the bull's-eye is accurate but not precise — is worth keeping in mind.

The difference between a measured value and the true value is called the error of the measurement. Random error scatters values above and below the true value and is reduced by averaging many measurements. Systematic error pushes all values in the same direction (a miscalibrated scale, a thermometer that reads 2 °C too high) and is not reduced by averaging — it must be diagnosed and corrected.

1.10 Uncertainty in Measurements

Every measured value carries an implied uncertainty in its last digit. When you read a graduated cylinder marked in 1-mL increments and report the volume as 27.4 mL, you are telling another chemist that you are sure of the 27 and that you estimated the .4. The .4 might really be a .3 or a .5; the 27 is reliable. The number of digits you report is therefore a public statement about how well you know the answer. We call those reported digits significant figures.

Worked Examples — Counting Significant Figures

EXAMPLE 1.5: Counting significant figures with leading and captive zeros

How many significant figures are in 0.004080 g?

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Identify and ignore the leading zeros ("0.00"). Rule 3: not significant.

Step 2 — Count the remaining digits: 4, 0, 8, 0.

Step 3 — Apply Rule 2: the captive 0 between 4 and 8 is significant.

Step 4 — Apply Rule 4a: the trailing 0 after the 8 is significant because the number contains a decimal point.

Step 5 — Total: 4 significant figures.

CHECK: All digits except the leading zeros count. ✓

EXAMPLE 1.6: Significant figures with no decimal point

How many significant figures are in 4500?

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Apply Rule 1 to the 4 and 5: 2 significant figures so far.

Step 2 — Apply Rule 4b to the trailing 0s: ambiguous.

Step 3 — Conclusion: the number has at least 2 and as many as 4 significant figures. Without context (or a decimal point), we cannot say for sure.

Step 4 — Best practice: rewrite the value in scientific notation to be explicit. 4.5 × 10³ = 2 sig figs; 4.50 × 10³ = 3 sig figs; 4.500 × 10³ = 4 sig figs.

CHECK: This is exactly the situation Rule 4b warns about — and exactly the situation scientific notation solves.

1.12 Significant Figures and Mathematical Operations

Once you can count significant figures, you must learn how to keep the right number of them after arithmetic. There are two rules — one for multiplication/division and one for addition/subtraction — and they are not the same. They are also among the most-broken rules in introductory chemistry, so go slowly here.

Rounding Off Numbers

Before stating the rules, we need a procedure for dropping unwanted digits.

  • If the first dropped digit is less than 5, drop it (round down). 7.43 → 7.4.
  • If the first dropped digit is greater than 5, raise the last kept digit by 1 (round up). 7.46 → 7.5.
  • If the first dropped digit is exactly 5 followed only by zeros (or nothing), the modern convention is "round to even" (also called banker's rounding): round so that the last kept digit is even. 7.45 → 7.4; 7.55 → 7.6. This convention prevents systematic upward bias.

Multiplication and Division

RULE: The result of a multiplication or division has the same number of significant figures as the input value with the fewest significant figures.

EXAMPLE 1.7: Multiplication with mixed sig fig counts

Calculate (4.18 × 12.0) ÷ 3.1 and report with the correct number of significant figures.

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Count sig figs of each input: 4.18 has 3, 12.0 has 3, 3.1 has 2.

Step 2 — Identify the least: 2 (from 3.1).

Step 3 — Compute the unrounded result: (4.18 × 12.0) ÷ 3.1 = 50.16 ÷ 3.1 = 16.18064…

Step 4 — Round to 2 sig figs: 16.

Show solution

16.

CHECK: One of the inputs (3.1) is the limiting factor. ✓

Addition and Subtraction

RULE: The result of an addition or subtraction has the same number of decimal places as the input value with the fewest decimal places (NOT the fewest significant figures).

EXAMPLE 1.8: Addition with different decimal places

Calculate 12.45 g + 3.2 g + 0.158 g.

SOLUTION

Step 1 — Identify the decimal places: 12.45 has 2, 3.2 has 1, 0.158 has 3.

Step 2 — Identify the fewest: 1 decimal place (from 3.2).

Step 3 — Compute the unrounded sum: 12.45 + 3.2 + 0.158 = 15.808 g.

Step 4 — Round to 1 decimal place: 15.8 g.

Show solution

15.8 g.

CHECK: 3.2 has only 1 decimal place, so the answer cannot be more precise than that. ✓

Significant Figures and Exact Numbers

Exact numbers (defined relationships, counted objects, integer multipliers) do not limit the number of significant figures in a calculated result. Only measured numbers do. When in doubt, ask: "could a more careful measurement change this number?" If no — exact. If yes — measured.

Multiplication by a Small Whole Number

If you multiply a measured value by a small whole-number multiplier (e.g., to find the total mass of three identical samples), the multiplier is exact and does not limit sig figs. The result has as many significant figures as the measured value. 3 × 4.18 g = 12.54 g (3 sig figs), not 12.5 g.

1.13 Expressing Numbers in Scientific Notation

Chemistry routinely involves very large numbers (Avogadro's number, 6.022 × 10²³) and very small numbers (the radius of a hydrogen atom, about 5.3 × 10⁻¹¹ m). Writing these out as ordinary decimals is unwieldy and ambiguous. Scientific notation solves both problems.

Anatomy of a Number in Scientific Notation

A number in scientific notation has the form N × 10ⁿ, where N is the coefficient (a number with magnitude between 1 and 10, that is, 1 ≤ |N| < 10) and n is an integer exponent. Examples: 4.18 × 10² is 418; 5.30 × 10⁻⁴ is 0.000530.

Converting Decimal → Scientific Notation

  1. Locate the decimal point and move it until exactly one non-zero digit is to its left.
  2. Count the number of places you moved it. That count becomes the exponent.
  3. If you moved the decimal to the LEFT, the exponent is positive. If you moved it to the RIGHT, the exponent is negative.

EXAMPLE 1.9: Converting a large decimal to scientific notation

Express 24,500,000 in scientific notation, assuming 3 significant figures.

Step 1 — Move the decimal point left until one non-zero digit remains to the left: 2.45.

Step 2 — Count the places moved: 7 places.

Step 3 — Assign sign: moved left → positive exponent.

Show solution

2.45 × 10⁷.

EXAMPLE 1.10: Converting a small decimal to scientific notation

Express 0.000 008 50 in scientific notation.

Step 1 — Move the decimal point right until one non-zero digit remains to its left: 8.50.

Step 2 — Count places moved: 6.

Step 3 — Sign: moved right → negative exponent.

Show solution

8.50 × 10⁻⁶.

CHECK: The number is small (< 1), so the exponent must be negative. ✓

Significant Figures in Scientific Notation

In scientific notation, every digit shown in the coefficient is significant. This is the cleanest way to record sig figs unambiguously. 1200 written as 1.2 × 10³ has 2 sig figs; 1.20 × 10³ has 3; 1.200 × 10³ has 4. The exponent does not affect the count.

1.14 Mathematical Operations in Scientific Notation

Multiplication

(A × 10ᵐ) × (B × 10ⁿ) = (A × B) × 10^(m+n). Multiply the coefficients and add the exponents.

EXAMPLE 1.11: Multiplication in scientific notation

Calculate (3.0 × 10⁴) × (2.5 × 10⁻²).

Step 1 — Multiply coefficients: 3.0 × 2.5 = 7.5.

Step 2 — Add exponents: 4 + (−2) = 2.

Step 3 — Combine: 7.5 × 10².

Step 4 — Sig figs: 3.0 has 2, 2.5 has 2, so 2 sig figs in answer. 7.5 already has 2 sig figs. ✓

Show solution

7.5 × 10² (= 750).

Division

(A × 10ᵐ) ÷ (B × 10ⁿ) = (A ÷ B) × 10^(m−n).

EXAMPLE 1.12: Division in scientific notation

Calculate (6.4 × 10⁵) ÷ (1.6 × 10²).

Step 1 — Divide coefficients: 6.4 ÷ 1.6 = 4.0.

Step 2 — Subtract exponents: 5 − 2 = 3.

Show solution

4.0 × 10³.

Addition and Subtraction

Before adding or subtracting, the two numbers must have the same exponent. Convert one of them so that they match, then add (or subtract) the coefficients and keep the common exponent.

EXAMPLE 1.13: Addition in scientific notation

Calculate (4.2 × 10³) + (3.1 × 10²).

Step 1 — Convert the smaller-exponent term to match the larger: 3.1 × 10² = 0.31 × 10³.

Step 2 — Add coefficients with matching exponents: 4.2 × 10³ + 0.31 × 10³ = 4.51 × 10³.

Step 3 — Apply the addition sig fig rule (decimal places, not sig figs). 4.2 × 10³ has 1 decimal place in the coefficient; 0.31 × 10³ has 2. Round to 1 decimal place: 4.5 × 10³.

Show solution

4.5 × 10³.

1.15 Using a Scientific Calculator

Every chemistry course assumes you can drive a scientific calculator confidently. Most numerical errors students lose points on are not chemistry mistakes — they are calculator-keystroke mistakes: a missing parenthesis around a denominator, a sign-change key (+/−) used in place of subtraction, or scientific notation typed as '× 10 ^' instead of with the dedicated EE / EXP / ×10ˣ key. This section is a short field guide.

Entering Numbers in Scientific Notation

Scientific calculators provide a dedicated key for the '× 10 to the' part of scientific notation, labeled EE, EXP, or ×10ˣ depending on the brand. To enter 6.022 × 10²³, press: 6 . 0 2 2 EE 2 3 . The display will read 6.022E23 or 6.022×10²³. Do NOT key it as 6.022 × 10 ^ 23 — that multiplies by 10 and then raises the result to the 23rd power, giving the wrong answer by a factor of 10.

For a negative exponent (e.g., 1.66 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, the mass of a proton), press the EE key first, then the (−) sign-change key, then the digits: 1 . 6 6 EE (−) 2 7 .

Order of Operations and Parentheses

Calculators follow standard mathematical precedence (PEMDAS), but they only know what you key in. The single most common error is forgetting that a fraction with multiple terms in the denominator needs parentheses around the entire denominator.

Example: to evaluate (4.50 × 22.4) ÷ (0.0821 × 298), key it as: ( 4.50 × 22.4 ) ÷ ( 0.0821 × 298 ) = . Without the second pair of parentheses, the calculator would compute (4.50 × 22.4) ÷ 0.0821 first, then multiply by 298 — an answer ~88,000× too large.

Square Roots, Squares, and Other Powers

Square root is usually a shifted (yellow / 2nd) function on √. Square is x² or simply the number followed by ^ 2. Other powers use yˣ or ^. Sign-change uses (−) or +/−, which is different from the subtraction key −.

Calculator Tips for Factor-Label Chains

When you do dimensional analysis (Chapter 2), enter the entire chain in one calculator expression rather than rounding intermediate results. 1.16 Graphs in Chemistry

A graph turns a column of numbers into a picture you can read at a glance. Chemists use graphs to discover trends, fit models, and report results. This section covers the skills you need: choosing axes, labelling them, plotting points, drawing best-fit lines, and interpreting slope and intercept.

Independent vs. Dependent Variables

The variable you control or vary is the independent variable; it goes on the horizontal (x) axis. The variable you measure in response is the dependent variable; it goes on the vertical (y) axis. Mnemonic: 'DRY MIX' — Dependent, Responding, Y-axis; Manipulated, Independent, X-axis.

Axes: Labels, Units, and Scale

Every axis must show: (1) what is plotted (the variable name), (2) the unit in parentheses or after a slash, and (3) numerical tick marks at regular intervals. Example axis labels: 'Mass (g)', 'Volume / mL', 'Temperature (°C)'. Choose a scale that uses most of the available graph area — if your data span 0 to 50 g, do not let the axis run from 0 to 500 g, leaving the points crammed in the bottom 10%.

The origin (0, 0) is conventional but not mandatory; if data cluster between 100 and 110 °C, an axis starting at 95 °C is acceptable as long as the break in the axis is shown.

Plotting Data and Drawing a Best-Fit Line

Mark each data point with a small dot or × at its (x, y) coordinates. If the relationship appears linear, draw a single straight best-fit line through the points using a ruler — do NOT connect the dots dot-to-dot. The best-fit line should pass as close as possible to all points, with roughly equal numbers of points above and below it. Real measurements scatter; the line is the trend, not the data.

Slope, Intercept, and Linear Relationships

A linear relationship has the form y = m·x + b, where m is the slope (rise / run) and b is the y-intercept (the y-value where the line crosses x = 0). To compute slope from a graph, pick two points on your best-fit line (NOT raw data points) far apart, read off (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂), and compute m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁). Slope carries units: (units of y) / (units of x).

A directly proportional relationship is the special case y = m·x where the line passes through the origin (b = 0); doubling x doubles y. A linear-but-not-proportional relationship has b ≠ 0. A nonlinear relationship is any curve — common ones in chemistry include the inverse curve PV = k (Boyle's law) and the exponential decay of radioactivity.

Reading Published Graphs

When you encounter a graph in a journal article or textbook: (1) read the axis labels and units first; (2) note the scale and any axis breaks; (3) identify the type of relationship (linear, proportional, exponential, …); (4) extract specific values by tracing horizontally and vertically from the curve to the axes; (5) check the figure caption for what the data represent and what was held constant.

EXAMPLE 1.16: Density from a mass-vs-volume plot

A student measures the mass of five aluminum cubes of different sizes. The data are tabulated below. Determine the density of aluminum from a graph of mass vs. volume.

Volume (cm³): 1.00, 2.00, 4.00, 8.00, 12.0

Mass (g): 2.70, 5.40, 10.80, 21.60, 32.4

Step 1 — Choose axes. Volume is the manipulated variable (x); mass is the responding variable (y).

Step 2 — Plot the data and draw the best-fit line. The points fall on a straight line passing through the origin → directly proportional, y = m·x.

Step 3 — Compute slope. Pick two points on the line: (2.00 cm³, 5.40 g) and (12.0 cm³, 32.4 g). m = (32.4 − 5.40) / (12.0 − 2.00) = 27.0 / 10.0 = 2.70 g/cm³.

Step 4 — Interpret. The slope of mass vs. volume is the density. Aluminum density ≈ 2.70 g/cm³, matching the literature value (2.70 g/cm³ at 20 °C).

When a relationship is directly proportional (passes through the origin), the slope IS the proportionality constant. This is how lab measurements of density, molar mass, ideal-gas constant R, Avogadro's number, and dozens of other constants are extracted from raw data.

Concepts to Remember

  1. Chemistry is the study of matter and the changes matter undergoes; it overlaps with physics, biology, geology, and astronomy at the boundaries.
  2. The scientific method is an iterative cycle of observation → question → hypothesis → experiment → analysis. It does not run in a straight line.
  3. Facts are observations; laws summarize patterns; hypotheses propose tentative explanations; theories are well-tested broad explanations. The everyday word "theory" is weaker than the scientific word.
  4. Science can address only falsifiable, empirical questions; ethical, aesthetic, and theological questions lie outside its scope.
  5. Every measurement carries a value AND a unit. Dropping units is the most preventable error in chemistry.
  6. Exact numbers (definitions, counts) have no uncertainty. Inexact (measured) numbers do.
  7. Accuracy is closeness to the true value; precision is reproducibility. They are independent qualities.
  8. Random error is reduced by averaging; systematic error is not — it must be diagnosed and corrected.
  9. The five rules for counting significant figures distinguish leading zeros (never significant), captive zeros (significant), and trailing zeros (significant if a decimal point is present).
  10. Multiplication and division: result has fewest sig figs of any input. Addition and subtraction: result has fewest decimal places of any input.
  11. Scientific notation expresses any number as N × 10ⁿ with 1 ≤ |N| < 10; every digit in N is significant.
  12. When adding or subtracting in scientific notation, first convert the numbers to share a common exponent.

On a graph, the manipulated variable goes on the x-axis and the responding variable on the y-axis (mnemonic: DRY MIX).

Key Terms Glossary

Accuracy (1.9) — How close a measured value is to the true (or accepted) value. A scale that reads 50.1 g for a 50.0 g standard is accurate to 0.1 g.

Chemistry (1.1) — The branch of natural science that studies matter — anything with mass and volume — and the changes matter undergoes.

Decimal place rule (1.12) — In addition or subtraction, the result has the same number of decimal places as the input with the fewest decimal places.

Exact number (1.8) — A number with no uncertainty, arising from a definition (e.g., 1 in = 2.54 cm) or from counting whole objects.

Fact, scientific (1.4) — A single, repeatable observation; the "what" without yet supplying the "why."

Hypothesis (1.4) — A tentative, falsifiable explanation for an observation, used to guide experimental tests.

Inexact number (1.8) — A number that comes from a measurement and so always carries some uncertainty in its last digit.

Law, scientific (1.4) — A concise statement (often an equation) that summarizes a large body of facts but does not explain them.

Measurement (1.7) — The assignment of a numerical value and a unit to a property of a sample.

Multiplication/division rule (1.12) — The result has the same number of significant figures as the input with the fewest.

Precision (1.9) — How close repeated measurements of the same quantity are to one another. High precision can coexist with low accuracy.

Random error (1.9) — Uncertainty that scatters values above and below the true value; reduced by averaging.

Scientific method (1.4) — The iterative cycle of observation, question, hypothesis, experiment, and analysis used to develop and revise scientific knowledge.

Scientific notation (1.13) — A way of writing numbers in the form N × 10ⁿ with 1 ≤ |N| < 10 and integer n; makes very large and very small numbers easier to read and unambiguous in significant figures.

Significant figures (1.11) — The digits in a measurement that carry meaningful information; the number of significant figures records how well the measurement is known.

Systematic error (1.9) — Error that pushes all measurements in the same direction; not reduced by averaging, only by recalibration or procedural correction.

Technology (1.2) — The application of scientific knowledge to build tools or solve practical problems.

Theory, scientific (1.4) — A well-tested broad explanation that ties together many facts, laws, and confirmed hypotheses; the strongest kind of explanation in science.

Uncertainty (1.10) — The estimated range within which the true value of a measured quantity lies; reflected in the last reported digit of a measurement.

Best-fit line (1.16) — A single straight line drawn through plotted data so that points scatter equally above and below it; not a dot-to-dot connection.

Dependent variable (1.16) — The quantity measured in response to a controlled change in the independent variable; plotted on the y-axis.

Independent variable (1.16) — The quantity the experimenter controls or varies; plotted on the x-axis.

Linear relationship (1.16) — A relationship of the form y = m·x + b that plots as a straight line.

Slope (1.16) — The rise/run ratio of a straight line; for chemistry data the slope often equals a physical constant (e.g., density, molar mass, R).

Y-intercept (1.16) — The value of y when x = 0; equals b in y = m·x + b.

This combined chapter is unusually long because it merges the original two introductory chapters of the course. Most instructors will spend two weeks on it. The following notes flag predictable points of student difficulty, suggest in-class activities, and list lab connections.

Common Student Difficulties

  • The hardest cell of the sig fig rules is the trailing-zero rule (Rule 4). Students consistently miscount 1200, 1200., and 1200.0. Spend extra board time on the difference.
  • Students confuse the addition rule (decimal places) with the multiplication rule (sig figs). Have them write the rule label next to every step in early problem sets.
  • Students think "exact" means "famous" or "published." Insist that an exact number must trace to a definition or a count.
  • "Theory" in everyday speech is much weaker than "theory" in science. Spend a few minutes early in the term making this difference vivid (e.g., theory of gravity vs. "my theory about my missing keys").

Recommended In-Class Activities

  • Card sort: print 20 statements on index cards and have small groups classify each as fact / law / hypothesis / theory. Debrief disagreements.
  • Hands-on accuracy and precision: provide three different balances and a calibration weight. Have each group record three measurements per balance and analyze for accuracy and precision.
  • Sig figs Jeopardy: a structured drill that mixes counting and arithmetic problems with light competition; effective in the second class meeting.

Lab Connections

  • Pair this chapter with a measurement-and-error lab: students measure the volume of an irregular object using both water displacement (graduated cylinder) and geometric calculation (caliper). Compare values and analyze sources of error.
  • A second lab tying scientific notation to data analysis: have students estimate the number of atoms in a copper penny by mass and connect that to the discussion of "very large numbers" that motivates Avogadro's number in Chapter 8.

Assessment Suggestions

  • Quiz at end of week 1: 10 short-answer items on classification of statements (fact/law/hypothesis/theory).
  • Quiz at end of week 2: 10 calculation items mixing sig fig counting, sig fig arithmetic, and scientific notation.
  • Recommended exam weight: 12–15% of total course grade for this combined chapter.
