Chapter Opener: Why Salt is Hard, Sugar Sweet, and Both Dissolve in Water

Drop a crystal of sodium chloride into a glass of water and it dissolves silently in seconds. Drop a sugar cube into the same glass and it dissolves too — also silently, also in seconds. From the outside, the two events look almost identical. But the chemistry inside the glass could not be more different. The salt has split into electrically charged sodium and chloride ions; the sugar has dissolved as intact, uncharged molecules. The reason is the difference between two kinds of chemical bond — ionic and covalent — and the difference is the subject of this chapter.

Bonding is the central organizing concept in chemistry. Once you can decide whether a compound is ionic or covalent, predict its formula, draw its Lewis structure, predict its three-dimensional shape using VSEPR, and decide whether it is polar — you can predict almost everything about how it will behave in a beaker. The work in this chapter is the work that lets the rest of chemistry happen.

Why this matters: Bonding is one of the most important concepts in this book for what comes after it. Lewis structures, VSEPR shapes, and polarity decide everything about how molecules behave: whether a drug can cross a cell membrane, why proteins fold the way they do, whether a battery electrolyte conducts ions, why ice floats. Organic chemistry is, in large part, a year of arrow-pushing on Lewis structures of the kind you draw in this chapter; pre-med biochemistry then builds another layer on top of those same diagrams. If a single chapter in this book is worth re-reading after exams, it is this one.

6.1 Types of Chemical Bonds

Three kinds of chemical bond are introduced in introductory chemistry:

  • Ionic bond. An electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, formed by the transfer of electrons from one atom to another. Typical of metal–nonmetal compounds (NaCl, MgO, CaCl₂).
  • Covalent bond. A pair of electrons shared between two atoms. Typical of nonmetal–nonmetal compounds (H₂O, CO₂, CH₄).
  • Metallic bond. A network of metal cations embedded in a "sea" of mobile valence electrons. Typical of pure metals and alloys (Fe, Cu, brass). We will revisit metallic bonding briefly in Chapter 10.

This chapter focuses on the first two.

6.2 Valence Electrons and Lewis Dot Symbols

Bonding involves only the electrons in the outermost shell — the valence electrons. For main-group elements, the number of valence electrons equals the group number in the older A/B numbering: Group IA = 1, IIA = 2, IIIA = 3, …, VIIIA = 8 (helium is an exception with 2). The IUPAC group number is less convenient: Groups 1, 2, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18 ↔ valence electrons 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 (subtract 10 from groups 13–18 for groups 1–18 of representative elements).

A Lewis dot symbol shows the chemical symbol of the element surrounded by dots representing valence electrons, one dot at a time on each of four sides (top, right, bottom, left), pairing only after all four sides have one dot. Examples (read across the four sides):

  • Na (1 valence): Na with one dot on the right → Na·
  • O (6 valence): four sides start filled with one dot each (4), then pairs are added → :Ö: with two pairs and two singles.
  • Cl (7 valence): three pairs and one single → :Cl·
  • Ne (8 valence): four pairs → :Ne:

6.3 The Octet Rule

The noble gases (except He) all share the valence configuration ns² np⁶ — eight valence electrons. Noble gases are also famously unreactive. Inferring the cause from the correlation, G. N. Lewis proposed in 1916 that other atoms gain stability by acquiring an octet of valence electrons, either by transferring electrons (forming ions) or by sharing them (forming covalent bonds). The octet rule has well-known exceptions (H, Li, Be, B, and many transition metals do not follow it; some elements in the third row and below can exceed it), but it is the right starting point for almost every Lewis structure in introductory chemistry.

HISTORICAL VIGNETTE — Gilbert N. Lewis (1875–1946): American physical chemist who, in two short papers (1916 and 1923), introduced the dot symbol, the octet rule, the idea of the shared electron pair, and the modern definition of acid and base. Lewis was nominated for the Nobel Prize 41 times and never received one. He is reported to have walked into his laboratory at Berkeley one afternoon, mixed a flask, and shortly after was found dead — possibly of a heart attack, possibly of an inhalation accident. His ideas, written before quantum mechanics existed, hold up extraordinarily well a century later.

6.4 The Ionic Bond Model

An ionic bond forms when a metal atom (with low ionization energy and low electronegativity) transfers one or more valence electrons to a nonmetal atom (with high electron affinity and high electronegativity). Both atoms then have noble-gas-like configurations and are oppositely charged; the electrostatic attraction between them is the ionic bond.

Example: Na (1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s¹) and Cl (1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁵). Na transfers its single 3s electron to the chlorine 3p subshell. The result is Na⁺ (1s² 2s² 2p⁶, like Ne) and Cl⁻ (1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶, like Ar). The attraction between Na⁺ and Cl⁻ is the ionic bond, and many such Na⁺/Cl⁻ pairs assemble into the rock-salt crystal structure.

6.5 Sign and Magnitude of Ionic Charge; Isoelectronic Species

Predicting an ion's charge from the periodic table:

  • Group 1 metals lose 1 electron → +1 (Li⁺, Na⁺, K⁺, …).
  • Group 2 metals lose 2 electrons → +2 (Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, …).
  • Group 13 metals lose 3 electrons → +3 (Al³⁺, …).
  • Group 15 nonmetals gain 3 electrons → −3 (N³⁻, P³⁻).
  • Group 16 nonmetals gain 2 electrons → −2 (O²⁻, S²⁻).
  • Group 17 nonmetals gain 1 electron → −1 (F⁻, Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻).

Transition-metal ions can vary in charge (Fe²⁺ vs. Fe³⁺, Cu⁺ vs. Cu²⁺); we will treat them in Chapter 7. For now, focus on main-group ions.

Two species with the same electron count are called isoelectronic. Na⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, F⁻, O²⁻, and N³⁻ are all isoelectronic with the noble gas Ne (10 electrons). Isoelectronic species share electron configuration, but they differ in nuclear charge and therefore in size: more protons → smaller radius.

6.6 Lewis Structures for Ionic Compounds

To draw the Lewis structure of an ionic compound: write the metal cation symbol with its charge (e.g., Na⁺) and write the nonmetal anion symbol with its charge in brackets, surrounded by 8 dots (an octet) and the charge: [:Cl:]⁻. Multiple ions of either type are shown as needed to balance charge. NaCl: Na⁺ [:Cl:]⁻. CaCl₂: Ca²⁺ paired with two [:Cl:]⁻ ions.

6.7 Chemical Formulas for Ionic Compounds

To write the formula for an ionic compound from the ion charges, balance the total positive charge with the total negative charge. The shortcut method (the "crisscross" rule): make the magnitude of each ion's charge become the subscript of the OTHER ion. Then reduce to lowest whole-number ratio.

EXAMPLE 6.1: Crisscross to find an ionic formula

Aluminum oxide: Al³⁺ and O²⁻.

Step 1 — Crisscross: Al gets a subscript of 2 (from oxygen's −2 magnitude); O gets a subscript of 3 (from aluminum's +3 magnitude).

Step 2 — Formula: Al₂O₃.

Step 3 — Check: 2(+3) + 3(−2) = +6 − 6 = 0 ✓.

Show solution

Al₂O₃.

EXAMPLE 6.2: Crisscross with reduction

Magnesium oxide: Mg²⁺ and O²⁻.

Step 1 — Crisscross: Mg₂O₂.

Step 2 — Reduce: divide both subscripts by their GCD (2): MgO.

Step 3 — Check: (+2) + (−2) = 0 ✓.

Show solution

MgO.

6.8 Structure of Ionic Compounds

Ionic compounds do not exist as isolated NaCl molecules; they form extended three-dimensional crystal lattices in which each ion is surrounded by oppositely charged neighbors. In rock salt (NaCl), each Na⁺ is surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ in an octahedral arrangement, and vice versa. The chemical formula NaCl tells you the simplest whole-number ratio of ions, not the size of any actual molecule.

6.9 Polyatomic Ions

A polyatomic ion is a group of covalently bonded atoms that carries a net electric charge. The atoms inside the polyatomic ion stick together as a unit; the unit as a whole has a charge. The most common polyatomic ions appear so frequently that they must be memorized.

6.10 The Covalent Bond Model

A covalent bond is a pair of valence electrons shared between two atoms. Both nuclei are simultaneously attracted to the shared electrons; the result is a region of electron density between them that holds the two atoms together. Most molecules in introductory chemistry — water, methane, CO₂, NH₃, glucose — are held together by covalent bonds. Pure covalent bonds form between atoms of similar electronegativity (typically two nonmetals).

6.11 Lewis Structures for Molecular Compounds

Lewis structures show every valence electron in a molecule, either as a bonding pair (a line between two atoms) or as a non-bonding (lone) pair (a pair of dots on a single atom).

6.12 Single, Double, and Triple Covalent Bonds

Atoms can share more than one pair of electrons. Two shared pairs make a double bond (e.g., C=O in CO₂); three shared pairs make a triple bond (e.g., N≡N in N₂). Multiple bonds are shorter and stronger than single bonds between the same two atoms.

6.13 Valence Electron Count and Number of Covalent Bonds

Most main-group nonmetals form a predictable number of covalent bonds equal to (8 − number of valence electrons), so that the total around the atom = 8. Hydrogen forms 1 bond (because it needs 2 to fill 1s²).

  • H: 1 bond
  • C (4 valence): 4 bonds
  • N (5 valence): 3 bonds (and 1 lone pair)
  • O (6 valence): 2 bonds (and 2 lone pairs)
  • F, Cl, Br, I (7 valence): 1 bond (and 3 lone pairs)

6.14 Coordinate Covalent Bonds

In most covalent bonds, each atom contributes one electron to the shared pair. In a coordinate covalent bond, both electrons of a shared pair come from the SAME atom. Once formed, a coordinate bond is indistinguishable from any other covalent bond. The classic example is the formation of NH₄⁺ from NH₃ and H⁺: nitrogen donates both electrons of its lone pair to bond to the bare proton.

6.15 Resonance Structures

Sometimes more than one valid Lewis structure can be drawn for the same molecule or ion, differing only in the placement of bonds and lone pairs (not in atom positions). The actual structure is best described as an average — a resonance hybrid — of the contributing Lewis structures. The classic example is the carbonate ion CO₃²⁻: the C–O double bond can be drawn to any of the three oxygens; the real ion has three equivalent C–O bonds, each with bond order ≈ 1⅓.

6.16 Systematic Procedure for Drawing Lewis Structures

  1. Count the total number of valence electrons in the molecule (or ion: add 1 per negative charge, subtract 1 per positive charge).
  2. Determine the central atom. The least electronegative atom (often the one written first in the formula) is usually central; never put H in the center.
  3. Connect the central atom to each peripheral atom by a single bond; this uses 2 electrons per bond.
  4. Distribute remaining electrons as lone pairs on peripheral atoms first, working toward octets (or duets for H).
  5. If the central atom has fewer than 8 electrons, convert one or more lone pairs from peripheral atoms into multiple bonds.
  6. Verify: every atom (except H) has 8 electrons; total electrons used = total counted in step 1.

EXAMPLE 6.3: Lewis structure of water (H₂O)

Step 1 — Valence electrons: H(1) × 2 + O(6) = 8.

Step 2 — O is central; H atoms are peripheral.

Step 3 — Single bonds: H–O–H uses 4 electrons.

Step 4 — Distribute 4 remaining electrons as 2 lone pairs on O.

Step 5 — Octet check: O has 8 (2 bonds × 2 + 2 lone pairs × 2). H atoms have 2 each (duets). ✓

STRUCTURE: H–Ö–H, with two lone pairs on O.

EXAMPLE 6.4: Lewis structure of carbon dioxide (CO₂)

Step 1 — Valence: C(4) + 2×O(6) = 16.

Step 2 — C central; O peripheral.

Step 3 — Single bonds C–O and C–O: 4 electrons used.

Step 4 — Distribute remaining 12 electrons as 3 lone pairs on each O (6 each = 12).

Step 5 — Octet check on C: only 4 electrons (2 bonds). Need to make multiple bonds.

Step 6 — Convert one lone pair on each O into a second C=O bond. Now: O=C=O with 2 lone pairs on each O.

Step 7 — Octet check: each O = 8 (2 bonds × 2 + 2 LP × 2); C = 8 (2 double bonds × 4). ✓

STRUCTURE: O=C=O with two lone pairs on each O.

EXAMPLE 6.5: Lewis structure with a triple bond — nitrogen (N₂)

Step 1 — Valence: 2 × 5 = 10.

Step 2 — Two atoms; nothing central.

Step 3 — Single bond N–N uses 2 electrons.

Step 4 — Distribute remaining 8 electrons as 2 lone pairs on each N.

Step 5 — Octet check: each N has only 4 (1 bond × 2 + 2 LP × 2 = 6 — wait: 2 + 4 = 6). Insufficient.

Step 6 — Convert two lone pairs (one from each N) into a triple bond. Now: :N≡N: with one lone pair per N.

Step 7 — Each N has 8 electrons (3 bonds × 2 + 1 LP × 2). ✓

STRUCTURE: :N≡N:

EXAMPLE 6.6: Lewis structure with resonance — carbonate ion CO₃²⁻

Step 1 — Valence: C(4) + 3×O(6) + 2 (charge) = 24.

Step 2 — C central; 3 O peripheral.

Step 3 — Three C–O single bonds: 6 electrons.

Step 4 — Distribute 18 remaining: 3 lone pairs on each O (3 × 6 = 18).

Step 5 — Octet check on C: 6 electrons (3 bonds). Need a multiple bond.

Step 6 — Convert one O lone pair into a C=O double bond.

Step 7 — Three equivalent locations for the double bond → THREE resonance structures, each contributing equally.

STRUCTURE: bracket the whole ion with 2− charge; show all three resonance forms with double-headed arrows between them.

6.17 Molecular Geometry — VSEPR Theory

Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory predicts the three-dimensional shape of a molecule from a simple postulate: electron pairs (whether bonding or lone) repel one another and arrange themselves around the central atom to be as far apart as possible. Count the "electron groups" around the central atom (each lone pair, each single bond, each double bond, and each triple bond counts as ONE group), look up the geometry for that count, then adjust for any lone pairs.

6.18 Electronegativity

Electronegativity is the relative ability of an atom in a bond to attract the shared electrons. Linus Pauling assigned the most electronegative element, fluorine, a value of 4.0 and worked downward from there. The trend on the periodic table is the opposite of metallic character: electronegativity increases up and to the right (excluding noble gases).

HISTORICAL VIGNETTE — Linus Pauling (1901–1994): Pauling, Caltech and later Stanford, won two unshared Nobel Prizes — the 1954 Chemistry prize for his work on the chemical bond, and the 1962 Peace Prize for his campaign against atmospheric nuclear-weapons testing. Pauling spent his later career arguing that high doses of vitamin C prevent and cure many diseases — a claim that did not survive scrutiny but illustrates how even great scientists can be wrong outside their specialty.

6.19 Bond Polarity

In a bond between two identical atoms (Cl–Cl, H–H), the two atoms pull on the shared electrons with equal force, and the electrons are shared equally — a nonpolar covalent bond. In a bond between two different atoms, the more electronegative atom pulls the shared electrons closer to itself, producing a partial negative charge (δ−) on that atom and a partial positive charge (δ+) on the other — a polar covalent bond. If the electronegativity difference is large enough, the electron pair is fully transferred and the bond is ionic.

Working guidelines:

  • ΔEN < 0.4: nonpolar covalent.
  • 0.4 ≤ ΔEN ≤ 1.7: polar covalent.
  • ΔEN > 1.7: ionic.

These thresholds are guides, not laws. Real bonds form a continuum from pure covalent to pure ionic, and the cutoffs blur in practice.

EXAMPLE 6.7: Classify a bond by electronegativity difference

Classify each bond as nonpolar covalent, polar covalent, or ionic:

  1. C–H,
  2. Na–Cl,
  3. O–H.

(a) ΔEN = |2.5 − 2.1| = 0.4 → on the boundary; usually called nonpolar covalent in practice.

(b) ΔEN = |3.0 − 0.9| = 2.1 → ionic.

(c) ΔEN = |3.5 − 2.1| = 1.4 → polar covalent (with O bearing δ−).

6.20 Molecular Polarity

A molecule is polar overall if (1) it contains polar bonds AND (2) those bonds do not cancel by symmetry. Bond polarities are vectors (they have direction); when the geometry of the molecule places polar bonds symmetrically opposite each other, their vectors cancel and the molecule is nonpolar even though every bond is polar.

Examples:

  • CO₂ — polar bonds (C=O), but linear geometry, vectors cancel, MOLECULE NONPOLAR.
  • H₂O — polar bonds (O–H), bent geometry, vectors do NOT cancel, MOLECULE POLAR.
  • CH₄ — slightly polar bonds (C–H), tetrahedral geometry, vectors cancel, MOLECULE NONPOLAR.
  • NH₃ — polar bonds (N–H), trigonal pyramidal geometry, vectors do not cancel, MOLECULE POLAR.

The lone pair on the central atom (in NH₃, H₂O) is itself a polarity contributor; you can think of it as adding a dipole vector that does not get canceled.

Polar — linear geometry but the two ends (H and N) differ in electronegativity (H 2.1, N 3.0); dipoles do not cancel.

19. Is BF₃ polar or nonpolar? B–F bonds are polar (ΔEN = 4.0 − 2.0 = 2.0).

Show solution

Trigonal planar; the three B–F dipoles point outward at 120° and cancel by symmetry. MOLECULE NONPOLAR.

20. Rank by increasing electronegativity: Na, C, O, F.

Show solution

Na (0.9) < C (2.5) < O (3.5) < F (4.0).

21. Which is shorter: a C–C single bond or a C=C double bond? A C=C or a C≡C triple bond?

Show solution

Multiple bonds are shorter and stronger: C≡C < C=C < C–C in length.

22. In which species is the C–O bond order exactly 1?

Show solution

CO₃²⁻'s individual C–O bonds in any one resonance form are not 1 — but the average over resonance is 4/3 ≈ 1.33. CH₃OH has a C–O bond order of 1.

Lewis Symbols, Octet, Ions

1. Draw Lewis symbols (in words: sym + dots) for: Na, Mg, Al, C, N, O, F, Ne.

Show solution

Na (1 dot), Mg (2 dots), Al (3 dots), C (4 dots), N (5 dots), O (6 dots), F (7 dots), Ne (8 dots, octet).

2. Predict the most likely ion charge for each: Li, Mg, Al, S, Cl, K.

Show solution

Li⁺, Mg²⁺, Al³⁺, S²⁻, Cl⁻, K⁺.

3. Why does sodium readily form Na⁺ but not Na²⁺ in chemical compounds?

Show solution

Na has one valence electron (3s¹). Removing it gives the noble-gas configuration of Ne; removing a second would require breaking into the very stable filled 2p shell (high IE2).

4. Why does Cl readily form Cl⁻ but not Cl²⁻?

Show solution

Cl gains one electron to complete the octet (matching Ar). A second electron would have to enter a higher-energy orbital and faces strong electron–electron repulsion in the already-saturated 3p shell.

5. State the octet rule and give one common exception.

Show solution

Many main-group atoms gain, lose, or share electrons to reach 8 valence electrons (s²p⁶). Common exceptions: H/He (duet); B and Al (often electron-deficient, 6 valence electrons); P, S, Cl in some compounds (expanded octet).

1. Predict the molecular geometry of CH₄.

Show solution

Tetrahedral, ~109.5° bond angles (4 bonding pairs, no lone pairs).

2. Predict the molecular geometry of H₂O.

Show solution

Bent (≈ 104.5°). 4 electron groups (2 bonds + 2 lone pairs); electron-pair geometry tetrahedral, molecular shape bent.

3. Predict the molecular geometry of NH₃.

Show solution

Trigonal pyramidal (≈ 107°). 4 electron groups (3 bonds + 1 lone pair).

4. Predict the molecular geometry of CO₂.

Electronegativity and Polarity

1. Rank in order of increasing electronegativity: Na, P, Cl, F.

Show solution

Na < P < Cl < F.

2. Predict whether each bond is nonpolar covalent, polar covalent, or ionic: H—H, H—Cl, Na—Cl, C—O.

Show solution

H—H nonpolar covalent (ΔEN = 0). H—Cl polar covalent (ΔEN ≈ 0.96). Na—Cl ionic (ΔEN ≈ 2.1). C—O polar covalent (ΔEN ≈ 0.89).

3. Identify whether CO₂ is polar or nonpolar overall and explain.

Show solution

Each C=O bond is polar, but the linear geometry causes the two bond dipoles to cancel. CO₂ is nonpolar overall.

4. Identify whether H₂O is polar or nonpolar overall and explain.

Show solution

Polar. Each O—H bond is polar, and the bent geometry leaves a net dipole moment.

5. Compare polarity of NH₃ vs. NF₃. Both are pyramidal; predict which has the larger dipole moment.

Show solution

NH₃ has the larger dipole. In NH₃ the bond dipoles point toward N and add with the lone-pair dipole; in NF₃ the bond dipoles point toward F (away from N) and partially oppose the lone-pair dipole.

Bond Order

1. State the bond orders in: H—H, O=O, N≡N.

Show solution

H—H = 1; O=O = 2; N≡N = 3.

2. Rank C—C, C=C, C≡C in order of increasing bond length.

Show solution

C≡C < C=C < C—C. Higher bond order = shorter, stronger bond.

3. Rank C—C, C=C, C≡C in order of increasing bond energy.

Show solution

C—C < C=C < C≡C.

4. Predict the bond order in the carbonate ion CO₃²⁻.

Show solution

Each C—O bond has bond order 4/3 (≈1.33), the average over three resonance structures with one double and two single bonds.

5. In O₃ (ozone), describe the bonding picture using resonance and give the average bond order.

Show solution

Two resonance structures place the double bond on either side. The average O—O bond order is 1.5; both bonds are equivalent in the real molecule.

Multi-Concept Problems

1. For the formate ion HCO₂⁻ (formula HCOO⁻):

  1. draw the Lewis structure(s),
  2. identify any resonance,
  3. predict the C bond angle,
  4. is the ion polar?
Show solution
  1. C central, bonded to H, to one O by double bond, to another O by single bond (the latter bears the negative charge).

2. The double bond can swap between the two O atoms → two equivalent resonance structures.

  1. Three electron groups (one is the H, two are O), no lone pair on C → trigonal planar, 120°.
  2. Yes, polar (the negative charge is delocalized but the H end is less electronegative).
  3. Compare the bond polarity of N–H in NH₃ with O–H in H₂O. Predict which molecule is more polar overall.
Show solution

ΔEN(N–H) = 3.0 − 2.1 = 0.9; ΔEN(O–H) = 3.5 − 2.1 = 1.4. O–H is more polar. H₂O is more polar overall (smaller bond angle further enhances dipole sum).

3. An unknown compound has formula AB₃ where A is in Period 2 and B is fluorine. The bond angles are all 120° and the molecule is nonpolar.

  1. Identify A.
  2. Draw the Lewis structure.
Show solution

120° angles + trigonal planar + nonpolar → no lone pair on A → 3 bonding groups, no lone pair → A has 3 valence electrons → Period 2, Group 13 → A = B (boron). Compound: BF₃. Lewis: B center with 3 single bonds to F; each F has 3 LP; B has only 6 e⁻ around it (octet exception).

4. H₂O and CO₂ both have polar bonds; one is polar and the other is not. Explain at the level of bond vectors.

Show solution

H₂O: bent shape, two O–H dipoles point outward at ~104.5°; their vector sum is non-zero → polar. CO₂: linear shape, two C=O dipoles point in opposite directions (180°), exactly cancel → nonpolar.

5. Why do salt and sugar both dissolve in water but only salt conducts electricity in solution?

Show solution

Salt (NaCl) is ionic — dissolves into mobile Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions, which carry charge. Sugar (sucrose) is molecular — dissolves into intact, neutral molecules; no free ions to carry charge.

6. Draw the Lewis structure of carbonate, CO₃²⁻, with all three resonance forms. Compute the average C–O bond order.

Show solution

Three forms with the C=O double bond on each O in turn. Bond orders in each form: one bond order 2, two bond orders 1. Average across three forms = (2+1+1)/3 = 4/3 ≈ 1.33.

7. An ionic compound made of cation X²⁺ and polyatomic ion Y³⁻ has formula X₃Y₂. Confirm formulation by charge balance and write a real example.

Show solution

Balance: 3(+2) + 2(−3) = +6 − 6 = 0 ✓. Example: Ca₃(PO₄)₂ — calcium phosphate (the major mineral component of bones and teeth).

8. Use VSEPR + electronegativity to predict whether each is polar: NH₃, BF₃, H₂S, CHCl₃.

Show solution

NH₃ — polar (trigonal pyramidal). BF₃ — nonpolar (trigonal planar; symmetric). H₂S — polar (bent, like H₂O). CHCl₃ — polar (tetrahedral but asymmetric: 1 H ≠ 3 Cl).

Concepts to Remember

  1. Three main bond types: ionic (transfer), covalent (sharing), metallic (electron sea).
  2. Octet rule: atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons (2 for H).
  3. Predict main-group ion charges from group number: 1 → +1, 2 → +2, 13 → +3, 15 → −3, 16 → −2, 17 → −1.
  4. Ionic compounds are written as smallest whole-number ratio of ions; balance + and − charges.
  5. Polyatomic ions stick together as a unit and carry net charge; memorize the common 15+ in Table 6.2.
  6. Covalent bonds are pairs of shared electrons; multiple bonds (double, triple) are shorter and stronger than single bonds.
  7. Lewis structures: count valence electrons, connect with single bonds, complete octets on outer atoms first, then on the central atom (introducing multiple bonds if needed).
  8. Resonance describes molecules whose true structure is best represented as the average of two or more equivalent Lewis structures.
  9. VSEPR: count electron groups around the central atom, then geometry follows from minimizing repulsion.
  10. Six common shapes: linear, trigonal planar, bent (3-group), tetrahedral, trigonal pyramidal, bent (4-group).
  11. Electronegativity increases up and to the right (excluding noble gases); F is the most electronegative element.
  12. ΔEN < 0.4 → nonpolar covalent; 0.4–1.7 → polar covalent; > 1.7 → ionic.
  13. A molecule is polar if it contains polar bonds AND those bonds do not cancel by symmetry.

Key Terms Glossary

Bonding pair (6.11) — A pair of valence electrons shared between two atoms in a covalent bond.

Coordinate covalent bond (6.14) — A covalent bond in which both shared electrons originate from the same atom.

Covalent bond (6.10) — A bond formed by the sharing of one or more electron pairs between two atoms.

Double bond (6.12) — A covalent bond consisting of two shared electron pairs.

Electronegativity (6.18) — The relative ability of an atom in a chemical bond to attract the shared electrons; highest for F, lowest for Cs and Fr.

Ionic bond (6.4) — An electrostatic attraction between cations and anions formed by electron transfer.

Ionic compound (6.7) — A compound consisting of cations and anions in a fixed ratio that balances charge; often a metal–nonmetal combination.

Isoelectronic (6.5) — Two species that have the same total number of electrons and identical electron configurations.

Lewis dot symbol (6.2) — A diagram of the chemical symbol surrounded by dots for valence electrons.

Lewis structure (6.11) — A diagram showing all valence electrons of a molecule as bonding pairs (lines) and lone pairs (dots).

Lone pair (6.11) — A pair of valence electrons not shared between atoms; localized on a single atom.

Octet rule (6.3) — The tendency of atoms to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons (2 for H).

Polar covalent bond (6.19) — A covalent bond between atoms of different electronegativity in which the electrons are shared unequally.

Polyatomic ion (6.9) — A group of covalently bonded atoms that carries a net electric charge.

Resonance structure (6.15) — One of two or more Lewis structures that differ only in the placement of electrons; the actual structure is best described as a hybrid.

Triple bond (6.12) — A covalent bond consisting of three shared electron pairs.

Valence electron (6.2) — An electron in the outermost shell of an atom; primary participant in bond formation.

VSEPR theory (6.17) — A predictive model in which electron groups around a central atom arrange to minimize repulsion.

Instructor Notes

Show Instructor Notes

Common Student Difficulties

  • Students confuse polar molecule with polar bond. Repeat the rule: polar molecule = polar bonds + asymmetric geometry.
  • Crisscross + reduce: students forget to reduce. (Mg₂O₂ → MgO is the classic miss.)
  • Polyatomic ions: students try to derive them. Insist that they be memorized; provide a printable card or Quizlet.
  • Lewis structures: students count electrons wrong by forgetting that each line is 2 electrons. Have them annotate every step.
  • VSEPR: students confuse electron geometry with molecular geometry. Distinguish them: lone pairs occupy electron-geometry positions but are not counted in molecular shape.

Recommended In-Class Activities

  • VSEPR with balloons: tie 2, 3, 4 balloons together at the knot to physically demonstrate linear, trigonal planar, tetrahedral shapes.
  • Molecular models: hand out small ball-and-stick kits and have students assemble CO₂, H₂O, NH₃, CH₄.
  • Polyatomic-ion bingo: review game using ion names and formulas.

Lab Connections

  • Conductivity of solutions: students test water, sugar solution, salt solution, and acid solutions with a simple bulb-and-battery probe; reinforces ionic vs. molecular distinction.
  • Crystal-growing lab: salt and sugar crystals visualized side-by-side; differences in shape reflect ionic lattice vs. molecular packing.
